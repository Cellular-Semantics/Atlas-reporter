# Migratory Dendritic Cells (MigDC)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Migratory dendritic cells (MigDCs), also referred to as "migratory dendritic cells" in this atlas (Anandasabapathy et al., 2014), represent a specialized subset of dendritic cells central to immune surveillance and modulation within human skin and its draining lymph nodes. They are characterized primarily by their high expression of MHC-II and CD11c, as well as additional surface markers that distinguish subsets (Woo et al., 2023; Dotta et al., 2024). Functionally, MigDCs are essential for transporting cutaneous antigens to lymph nodes, thus bridging peripheral immune events with central immune activation. Notably, their biology includes not only antigen transport and presentation, but also immune regulation, including the promotion of tolerance under certain microenvironmental conditions (Lindenberg et al., 2013).

## Markers

MigDCs are defined and subtyped using a combination of surface markers and gene expression profiles:

- **MHC-II (major histocompatibility complex II):** High expression underscores efficient antigen presentation to T cells (Woo et al., 2023).
- **CD11c:** Elevated levels mark dendritic cell lineage and readiness for migration and antigen presentation (Woo et al., 2023).
- **CCR7:** This chemokine receptor is essential for LN-homing following antigen acquisition in the skin. Its presence distinguishes migratory DCs and is required for their trafficking to lymph nodes (Caro et al., 2022).
- **LAMP3:** Expressed specifically in humans, LAMP3 aids in distinguishing mature migratory DC states (Caro et al., 2022).
- **CD103 (Integrin αE):** Marks migratory cDC1-like subsets, facilitating tissue egress (Dotta et al., 2024).
- **Langerin (CD207):** Used for identification of Langerin+ subsets among MigDCs, relevant to antigen capture and migration (Mollah et al., 2013).
- **CD205:** High expression, particularly after Flt3L stimulation, correlates with superior antigen uptake capacity (Mollah et al., 2013; Anandasabapathy et al., 2014).
- **CD301b (MGL2):** Found especially in cDC2-like migratory populations, indicating further functional specialization (Weckel et al., 2022; Dotta et al., 2024).
- **CD14+CD141+DC-SIGN+** (in humans): Marks immature, regulatory-prone migratory DCs, with low co-stimulatory molecules, in the context of IL-10 signaling (Lindenberg et al., 2013).
- **CACNB3 (Cavβ3):** Distinguishes migDCs by enabling ATP-dependent migration in response to tissue damage (Woo et al., 2023).

The significance of these markers extends beyond identification: for example, CCR7's presence is directly tied to the migration pathway required for skin-to-LN antigen delivery, while MHC-II and co-stimulatory molecules (such as CD80 and CD86) influence the cell's ability to activate various T cell subsets. The expression of certain inhibitory molecules (such as PD-L1, CD163) under tolerogenic conditions demonstrates the regulatory flexibility within MigDC populations (Lindenberg et al., 2013).

## Location

### Skin and Draining Lymph Nodes

MigDCs are resident in non-lymphoid tissues, primarily the skin's dermal compartments, and migrate via afferent lymphatic vessels to the skin-draining lymph nodes (Mollah et al., 2013; Woo et al., 2023).

> "MigDCs are non-lymphoid tissue DCs that can migrate to the tdLN via the lymphatic system rather than the blood."
>
> — Caro et al. (2022)

Within the lymph node compartment, MigDCs are present primarily as MHC-II^hi CD11c^int cells, and their subsets can be delineated by expression profiles of Langerin, CD11b, CD103, and related markers (Zhang et al., 2022; Dotta et al., 2024).

### Developmental and Experimental Context

MigDCs are identified and expanded under conditions such as Flt3L stimulation, inflammation, or vaccination (Mollah et al., 2013; Anandasabapathy et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2022), and their abundance or phenotype can be further modified by the tissue microenvironment—such as in the presence of suppressive cytokines like IL-10 (Lindenberg et al., 2013).

## Function

### Antigen Capture and Transport

MigDCs constitutively patrol the skin, capturing antigens and efficiently transporting them to draining lymph nodes.

> "Skin DC carry antigens and constitutively migrate to the skin draining lymph nodes (LN)."  
> — Mollah et al. (2013)

> "migDCs rapidly transport FITC from skin to skin draining LN."
>
> — Mollah et al. (2013)

> "FITC+ve cells in the lymph node had the phenotype of migratory DC subsets, which we infer had transported FITC from skin to LN."
>
> — Chandra et al. (2016)

MigDCs display a heightened efficiency in antigen capture compared to resident DC populations in lymph nodes.

> "MigDCs captured antigen with greater efficiency than cDCs by ... label in the proximal LNs (popliteal), irrespective of Flt3L treatment"
>
> — Anandasabapathy et al. (2014)

### T Cell Activation and Immune Modulation

After migration to the lymph node, MigDCs present captured antigens to T cells in the T-cell zones, driving both CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses:

> "migDCs loaded with antigen migrate to the T-cell zones of the LNs wherein they can prime CD4 + and CD8 + T cells."
>
> — Caro et al. (2022)

MigDCs upregulate MHC-II and co-stimulatory molecules during migration, supporting pro-inflammatory immune responses under appropriate stimulatory conditions.

> "This migratory phenotype is also characterized by the upregulation of MHC-II, costimulatory molecules, and secretion of inflammatory cytokines, which further drive the inflammatory T-cell responses [58,74]."
>
> — Caro et al. (2022)

However, MigDCs also play significant roles in immune regulation and tolerance. Environmental cues (e.g., IL-10) can push certain MigDC subsets toward a regulatory, tolerogenic phenotype:

> "IL-10 showed a unique ability to induce predominant migration of an immature CD14+CD141+DC-SIGN+ DC subset with low levels of co-stimulatory molecules, up-regulated expression of the co-inhibitory molecule PD-L1 and the M2-associated macrophage marker CD163"
>
> — Lindenberg et al. (2013)

> "migration of immature CD14+ DDC was accompanied by increased release of IL-10, poor expansion of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, and skewing of Th responses to favor coordinated FoxP3 and IL-10 expression and regulatory T cell differentiation and outgrowth"
>
> — Lindenberg et al. (2013)

Transcriptomic analysis in mouse and human MigDCs further reveals shared gene signatures associated with dampening of DC and T cell activation.

> "Transcriptomics in mouse and human reveal migDC subsets from skin relate most closely to each other and share gene signatures related to dampening of DC and T cell activation."
>
> — Anandasabapathy et al. (2014)

Moreover, depletion or blockade of migratory DCs can paradoxically lead to enhanced immune priming:

> "Impairing DC migration from skin to the sdLN via knockout of the CCR7 receptor and deletion of migDC subsets including Langerin + CD103 + DCs enhanced, not diminished, immune priming."
>
> — Anandasabapathy et al. (2014)

### Antigen Transfer and Cooperation with Resident DCs

MigDCs can deliver antigen directly to lymph node-resident DCs, further broadening T cell priming capacity.

> "migDCs can also transfer their antigens to the LN resident DC, which can prime T cells [74]."
>
> — Caro et al. (2022)

### Migration Mechanisms

MigDC egress from tissues and subsequent LN entry is dependent on chemokine (CCR7)-mediated signaling pathways.

> "steady-state migration of DCs from the skin to the draining LN depends on expression of the chemokine receptor CCR7."
>
> — Anandasabapathy et al. (2014)

> "Mice that lack CCR7 demonstrate a complete absence of migDCs in the LN"
>
> — Anandasabapathy et al. (2014)

ATP-dependent signaling, governed by the voltage-gated calcium channel subunit CACNB3, is essential for their migration in the context of tissue injury and inflammation.

> "migDCs can be distinguished from other DC subsets and immune cells by their expression of the voltage-gated calcium channel subunit \u03b23 (Cav\u03b23; CACNB3), which exclusively facilitates ATP-dependent migration in vitro and during tissue damage in vivo."
>
> — Woo et al. (2023)

## Structure / Morphology

Evidence on the specific structure or morphology of MigDCs is limited. Available descriptions focus primarily on the phenotypic characterization using flow cytometry and surface marker expression, with subsets discriminated by combinations of MHC-II, CD11c, Langerin, CD11b, CD103, CD301b, and CD205 (Mollah et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2022; Woo et al., 2023). No direct ultrastructural or detailed morphological data were provided in the traversed literature.
