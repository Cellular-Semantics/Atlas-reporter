# c5 (CCL18⁺ Macrophages / Alveolar Macrophage Subset)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Cluster c5, according to the referenced skin atlas and supplementary findings, represents a population of CCL18⁺ macrophages, with additional marker genes MARCO and RNASE2, and possible functional alignment with a subset of alveolar macrophages (Supplementary Table 2, atlas). This cell type is identified in adult human skin and is implicated in immune surveillance and modulation, particularly in skin inflammation and tissue injury contexts. Multiple lines of evidence from mouse and human studies highlight the central role of the complement pathway—especially the C5/C5a axis—in skin inflammatory responses, neutrophil recruitment, and pain, processes in which macrophages are key effectors.

## Markers

- **CCL18:** Identified as an enriched marker gene for cluster c5 and frequently used to denote distinct macrophage populations in tissue, particularly those associated with anti-inflammatory roles and tissue remodeling (Supplementary Table 2, atlas).
- **MARCO:** Another cluster-defining marker, MARCO is a scavenger receptor usually expressed on macrophages and involved in pattern recognition and tissue homeostasis (Supplementary Table 2, atlas).
- **RNASE2:** A known eosinophil marker with roles in antiviral defense and immune modulation, identified as an additional marker of c5 (Supplementary Table 2, atlas).

These markers jointly indicate a specialized skin macrophage phenotype, likely tailored for immune surveillance and response within the adult skin microenvironment (Supplementary Table 2, atlas).

## Function

Macrophages of the c5 cluster, as denoted by their CCL18⁺ status and complement involvement, are positioned as central players in skin immune regulation, notably through their participation in inflammatory cascades, host defense, and tissue injury responses.

### Inflammation and Immune Modulation

> "Local complement activation drives C5a generation associated with neutrophil recruitment and activation resulting in skin lesions and inflammation."
>
> — Laffer et al. (2025)

> "AP pathway-driven generation of C5a/C5adesArg is a critical driver of neutrophil activation and subsequent skin lesion development (detailed in Figure 6)."
>
> — Laffer et al. (2025)

> "In different BP models, C5/C5a have been found to attract and activate neutrophils (25) and mast cells (26)."
>
> — Laffer et al. (2025)

These findings connect complement C5 and its split product, C5a, to immune cell activation in the skin—most relevantly for CCL18⁺ macrophages, which act as both sensors and effectors in these inflammatory networks.

### Pain and Response to Injury

> "C5a production in the skin after incision was directly measured as well."
>
> — Jang et al. (2011)

Increased local C5 and C5a after injury supports a role for these cells in wound healing, pain, and inflammatory signaling.

### Disease Contexts

The C5a/C5aR1 pathway is critical in autoimmune and inflammatory skin diseases:

> "blocking C5a/C5aR1 signaling remarkably attenuates IMQ-induced psoriasiform skin inflammation"
>
> — Zheng et al. (2020)

> "the C5a/C5aR1 pathway plays an essential role in the pathogenesis of psoriasis."
>
> — Zheng et al. (2020)

> "C5 or combined AP/C5 inhibition by M014 but not C5a/C5adesArg-targeting by M031 reduced the development of skin lesions and dermal/epidermal separation in the setting of therapeutic treatment."
>
> — Laffer et al. (2025)
