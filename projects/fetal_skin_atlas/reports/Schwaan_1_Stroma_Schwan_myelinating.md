# Myelinating Schwann Cell in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [myelinating Schwann cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0002573) (CL:0002573, exact match)

## Summary

Myelinating Schwann cells (Schwaan_1_Stroma_Schwan_myelinating, using the atlas-assigned 'Schwaan' spelling) are one of two Schwann cell subtypes identified by Reynolds et al. (2021) in adult human skin. They are specialised glial cells of the peripheral nervous system that wrap large-diameter myelinated axons in compact myelin sheaths, enabling rapid saltatory nerve conduction. In adult skin, myelinating Schwann cells are defined by high expression of myelin structural proteins MBP (myelin basic protein), MPZ (myelin protein zero, P0), and PLP1 (proteolipid protein 1). They are found along myelinated sensory nerve fibres (A-beta mechanoreceptor fibres) in the dermis and are distinguished from non-myelinating Remak Schwann cells (Schwaan_2) by their myelin gene expression signature.

## Markers

Defining markers of myelinating Schwann cells:

> "Myelinating Schwann cells are characterised by high expression of MPZ, MBP and PLP1, which encode the major structural proteins of compact peripheral myelin"
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

MPZ as the most abundant PNS myelin protein:

> "MPZ (P0) is the most abundant protein in peripheral nervous system (PNS) myelin, comprising over 50% of total PNS myelin protein, and is exclusively expressed in myelinating Schwann cells"
>
> — Ioghen et al. (2020)

The distinction between the two Schwann cell subtypes in skin:

> "Two Schwann cell subtypes exist in adult human skin: myelinating (MPZ+/MBP+) and non-myelinating (NGFR+/GFAP+/GLUL+)"
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

Key markers: **MPZ** (myelin protein zero/P0, most abundant PNS myelin protein), **MBP** (myelin basic protein), **PLP1** (proteolipid protein 1), **S100B** (pan-Schwann cell marker), **PRX** (periaxin, myelinating Schwann cell marker).

## Location

Myelinating Schwann cells are found along the large myelinated nerve fibres (primarily A-beta sensory fibres) that run in nerve bundles throughout the dermis. Each myelinating Schwann cell wraps a single axon segment between two consecutive nodes of Ranvier, forming one internode of compact myelin.

## Function

### Myelin formation and saltatory conduction

Myelinating Schwann cells produce compact myelin sheaths around large-diameter peripheral nerve axons, enabling saltatory conduction of action potentials from node to node. This dramatically increases nerve conduction velocity compared to unmyelinated fibres. MPZ mediates compaction of myelin bilayers through homophilic adhesion, while MBP stabilises the major dense line of compact myelin.

### Axon trophic support

Myelinating Schwann cells provide metabolic and trophic support to the axons they ensheath, and are required for long-term axon maintenance. Disruption of Schwann cell-axon interactions leads to axonal degeneration even in the absence of myelin loss.

### Response to nerve injury

Following peripheral nerve injury, myelinating Schwann cells dedifferentiate into a repair phenotype, downregulate myelin genes, and upregulate NGFR and GDNF to support axonal regrowth (Wallerian degeneration response). This repair programme involves partial re-expression of markers associated with the non-myelinating state.
