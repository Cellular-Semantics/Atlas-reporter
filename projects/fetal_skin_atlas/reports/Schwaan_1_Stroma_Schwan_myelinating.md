# Schwann Cell (Myelinating)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Myelinating Schwann cells are specialized glial cells of the peripheral nervous system responsible for ensheathing large-diameter axons in a one-to-one relationship and producing the myelin sheath critical for rapid nerve impulse transmission. In skin, these cells reside principally in association with peripheral nerves within the dermis and hypodermis, where they can be identified by expression of canonical Schwann cell and myelin-associated markers, including MBP, MPZ, PLP1, and PRX, among others. Their identity in single-cell transcriptomic atlases draws from robust marker evidence and functional studies. Beyond their myelinating role, these cells display notable plasticity, including the capacity to dedifferentiate into repair phenotypes after injury and, under certain conditions, revert to multipotency. Their origin can encompass neural crest and, as recently shown, mesenchymal-lineage derivatives from the skin, underscoring their developmental flexibility.

## Markers

Myelinating Schwann cells are defined by a suite of markers related to myelination, Schwann cell identity, and function:

- **Krox20 (EGR2)**: As a transcription factor, Krox20 is upregulated in promyelinating Schwann cells and required for the onset of myelination.
  
  > "As differentiation progresses, myelination onset markers, such as Krox20, start to be expressed during a pro-myelinating state, in which cells are not yet actively producing myelin. The latter is indicated by the expression of the mature myelination marker MBP."
  >
  > — Hörner et al. (2022)

- **MBP (Myelin Basic Protein)**: A major structural protein integral to the formation of compact myelin in the peripheral nervous system.
  
  > "After 2 days of culture in differentiation medium, Schwann cells showed a differentiated cell phenotype, characterized by increased expression of pro-myelinating transcription factor Oct6 protein, myelinating regulator transcription factor Krox20 protein and mRNA (Figs 1a and b, Supplementary Figs S1 and S2), and the major myelin proteins MBP protein and mRNA (Fig. 1b,c and Supplementary Fig. S2)."
  >
  > — Imai et al. (2017)
  
  > "In the subcutaneous nerves, some of the traced cells expressed the myelin marker MBP ( Figure 3B), indicating that they were myelinating Schwann cells."
  >
  > — Gresset et al. (2015)

- **MPZ (Myelin Protein Zero)** and **PLP1 (Proteolipid Protein 1)**: Major myelin proteins specific to myelinating Schwann cells, essential for myelin sheath structure and compaction.
  
  > "Postnatally, immature Schwann cells give rise to myelinating Schwann cells that not only express pan-Schwann markers ErbB3 and S100 but also express myelin proteins such as myelin basic protein (Mbp), myelin protein zero (Mpz), and proteolipid protein 1 (Plp1)"
  >
  > — Zhang et al. (2025)

- **PRX (Periaxin)**: Crucial for peripheral nerve myelin maintenance.
  
  > "Myelinating Schwann cells express PRX (Periaxin), an essential gene for the maintenance of peripheral nerve myelin"
  >
  > — Yang et al. (2025)

- **S100, ErbB3**: Pan-Schwann cell markers, with S100 as a Ca2+-binding protein and ErbB3 as a neuregulin receptor.
  
  > "Postnatally, immature Schwann cells give rise to myelinating Schwann cells that not only express pan-Schwann markers ErbB3 and S100 but also express myelin proteins such as myelin basic protein (Mbp), myelin protein zero (Mpz), and proteolipid protein 1 (Plp1)"
  >
  > — Zhang et al. (2025)

- **CASPR**: Detected alongside MBP as evidence of a myelinating phenotype in SKP-derived Schwann cells.

  > "cell bodies positive for human mitochondrial antigen were aligned along MBP-positive myelin sheaths that were associated with the paranodal protein CASPR ( Figure 7I), consistent with a myelinating Schwann cell phenotype. Thus, human foreskinderived SKPs can generate bona fide Schwann cells that myelinate axons both in culture and in vivo."
  >
  > — Krause et al. (2014)

- **Loss of ColVI expression**: Myelinating Schwann cells do not express Collagen VI, distinguishing them from immature Schwann cells.

  > "once Schwann cells acquire their mature myelinating phenotype, they cease to express ColVI (Fig. 2) (Vitale et al., 2001)."
  >
  > — Gregorio et al. (2018)

These markers, particularly MBP, MPZ, PLP1, and PRX, are not only identifiers but are functionally important for myelin formation, stabilization, and maintenance.

## Location

### Skin and Cutaneous Nerves

Myelinating Schwann cells are consistently identified in peripheral nerves of adult and prenatal human skin. They are present in the dermal and subcutaneous nerve trunks:

> "In the subcutaneous nerves, some of the traced cells expressed the myelin marker MBP ( Figure 3B), indicating that they were myelinating Schwann cells."
>
> — Gresset et al. (2015)

This localization aligns with single-cell atlases and fate-mapping studies demonstrating that myelinating Schwann cells are intimately associated with nerve fibers throughout the dermis and hypodermis.

### Extracutaneous and Developmental Context

These cells also originate from various embryonic sources and can arise from both neural crest and non-neural crest (e.g., mesenchymal) precursors, particularly in skin-derived cultures:

> "nonneural crest dermal mesenchymal cells can generate myelinating Schwann cells that are highly similar to nerve-derived Schwann cells."
>
> — Krause et al. (2014)

> "the human foreskin SKPs make myelinating Schwann cells."
>
> — Krause et al. (2014)

This expands their spatial and developmental context beyond classic neural crest derivatives.

## Function

### Myelination and Saltatory Conduction

Myelinating Schwann cells are the exclusive generators of myelin in peripheral nerves, critical for efficient propagation of action potentials:

> "Myelinating Schwann cells spirally wrap large diameter axons, form compact myelin lamellae around axons, produce mature myelin sheaths, and thus contribute to the rapid conduction of nerve impulses."
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

> "Peripheral axons transmit general somatic information, including motor signals, in adult vertebrates and are myelinated by Schwann cells."
>
> — Park and Feltri (2011)

### Support, Homeostasis, and Microenvironment Regulation

They are key players in supporting, protecting, and regulating the neuron’s microenvironment:

> "These glial cell types have distinct roles in supporting, protecting, and regulating the microenvironment of PNS neurons [10,40,41]."
>
> — Yang et al. (2025)

> "marker genes are associated with biological processes, such as synaptic regulation, ion transmembrane transport, axon guidance and myelination"
>
> — Yang et al. (2025)

### Regeneration and Repair

Myelinating Schwann cells are highly plastic, capable of dedifferentiation after nerve injury to aid regeneration and potentially reverting to multipotency:

> "Injury of peripheral nerves causes dedifferentiation of both types of mature Schwann cells (myelinating and nonmyelinating) into repair Schwann cells [46][47][48] . Our pseudotime trajectory calculations suggest that myelinating and nonmyelinating Schwann cells of the skin dedifferentiate into profibrotic, repair-like Schwann cells in keloids."
>
> — Direder et al. (2022)

> "we demonstrated successful cellular reprogramming of adult myelinating Schwann cells into an immature multipotent NCSC phenotype [21]."
>
> — Widera et al. (2012)

> "Following the resolution of nerve damage, repair Schwann cells re-differentiate into mature myelinating and non-myelinating Schwann cells."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

> "prior mature myelinating Schwann cells can assume a mature non-myelinating phenotype and vice versa [105,106]."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

### Developmental Role and Plasticity

During development and regeneration, myelinating Schwann cells emerge from proliferating precursors and can derive from non-myelinating Schwann cells:

> "In experimentally induced allergic neuritis, myelinating Schwann cells are destroyed, but can be effectively regenerated (Saida et al., 1980)."
>
> — Garratt et al. (2000)

> "This regenerative capacity is particularly evident in mice that express diphtheria toxin A-chain in myelinating Schwann cells. In such animals, myelinating Schwann cells die and are continuously regenerated through proliferation of the nonmyelinating compartment (Messing et al., 1992)."
>
> — Garratt et al. (2000)

They also play a key role during nerve repair through re-differentiation from the repair phenotype after injury:

> "In the later phase of the regenerative process, they differentiate into myelinating and non-myelinating Schwann cells, determining the recovery of nerve morphology."
>
> — El Soury et al. (2018)

### Response to Neuregulin-1 (NRG1)

NRG1 signaling is specifically crucial for developmental myelination and effective remyelination:

> "in myelinating Schwann cells, NRG1 is required for developmental myelination and acts to accelerate the early phase of remyelination during repair"
>
> — Fricker et al. (2013)

### Capacity for Therapeutic Generation

Myelinating Schwann cells can be generated in vitro from skin-derived precursors (SKPs), and transplanted SKP-derived Schwann cells interact with and myelinate axons in vivo:

> "McKenzie et al. (2006) transplanted neonatal SKP-derived Schwann cells into crushed sciatic nerves of mice. These cells successfully associated with and myelinated regenerating axons."
>
> — Bajada et al. (2008)

## Structure / Morphology

Myelinating Schwann cells have unique ultrastructural and organizational attributes within peripheral nerves:

> "Myelinating Schwann cells, the focus of this discussion, exhibit radial and longitudinal polarization. As myelination progresses, Schwann cells organize into distinct domains, each with a unique protein array and a set of interconnected cytoplasmic compartments. Longitudinal polarity is prominently manifested in the overall organization of myelinating Schwann cells and axons, forming distinct nodal, paranodal, and internodal compartments."
>
> — Jiang et al. (2024)

They ensheath single large-diameter axons (1:1 ratio), producing compact myelin lamellae:

> "Myelinating Schwann cells develop through a further intermediate state, termed a promyelinating Schwann cell, which establishes a 1:1 relationship with an axon sorted for myelination [84]."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

> "Myelinating Schwann cells spirally wrap large diameter axons, form compact myelin lamellae around axons, produce mature myelin sheaths..."
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

Both axonal sorting and myelination involve dramatic changes in Schwann cell morphology, including protrusions of the plasma membrane.
