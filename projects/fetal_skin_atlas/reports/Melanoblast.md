# Melanoblast in Fetal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Source dataset: Gopee et al. (2024), prenatal skin, 7–17 PCW
Cell Ontology: [melanoblast](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000541) (CL:0000541, exact match)

## Summary

The Melanoblast cluster in the Gopee et al. (2024) prenatal skin atlas represents neural crest-derived melanocyte precursor cells present in fetal skin from 7 to 17 PCW. Melanoblasts are identified by expression of the master regulator MITF, the receptor tyrosine kinase KIT, and early melanocyte lineage markers SOX10 and DCT, but lack the mature melanogenic enzymes (TYR, TYRP1, PMEL) that characterise fully differentiated melanocytes. In fetal skin, melanoblasts migrate from neural crest tissues through the dermis toward the epidermis and developing hair follicles. The atlas captured melanoblasts as a distinct population within the broader melanocyte lineage alongside the differentiated melanocyte state. The melanocyte lineage is among those that mature most rapidly toward adult-like states: in the skin organoid (SkO) model, alignment with adult melanocyte cell states was detected as early as 4 weeks of culture.

## Markers

### Lineage-defining transcription factors: MITF and SOX10

Melanoblasts are defined by expression of MITF (microphthalmia-associated transcription factor) and SOX10, which together drive the melanocyte lineage from neural crest progenitors. MITF is necessary for both melanoblast survival and subsequent melanocyte differentiation.

### Early differentiation markers: DCT and KIT

Dihydroxyphenylalanine trichrome (DCT, also TYRP2) and the receptor tyrosine kinase KIT are expressed in melanoblasts prior to full activation of the melanogenic programme. KIT signalling through its ligand KITLG is required for melanoblast survival and migration in the dermis.

### Absence of mature melanogenic enzymes

Melanoblasts are distinguished from differentiated melanocytes by absence or low expression of mature melanogenic enzymes TYR and TYRP1, and the melanosomal structural protein PMEL. Upregulation of these proteins marks the transition from melanoblast to melanocyte.

### Rapid maturation trajectory in organoid models

> "By contrast, accelerated differentiation was observed in keratinocytes and melanocytes, with alignment to adult cell states seen as early as 4 weeks of SkO culture"
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

## Location

### Dermis and epidermis of fetal skin, 7–17 PCW

Melanoblasts are annotated as a fine-grained fetal cell type in Gopee et al. (2024) (Supplementary Table 6), present throughout the 7–17 PCW sampling window. At early time points melanoblasts are found as migrating cells in the dermis; as development proceeds they colonise the basal layer of the interfollicular epidermis and the outer root sheath of developing hair follicles.

## Function

### Precursor to skin and hair follicle melanocytes

The primary function of melanoblasts is to serve as a migratory precursor pool that establishes the melanocyte population in fetal skin. After colonising the epidermis and hair follicles, melanoblasts differentiate into melanocytes that produce melanin and transfer it to surrounding keratinocytes. The melanocyte lineage is among the ectoderm-derived lineages in this atlas that achieve adult-like maturation earliest in development.
