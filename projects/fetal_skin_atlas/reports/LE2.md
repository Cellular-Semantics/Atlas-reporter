# Lymphatic Endothelial 2 (LE2)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Lymphatic Endothelial 2 (LE2) is a fine-grained subtype of lymphatic endothelial cells (LECs) present in the skin, as identified in both prenatal and adult skin single-cell datasets. These cells are characterized by established lymphatic markers such as PROX1, PDPN (podoplanin), and LYVE1, as well as a distinct set of differentially expressed genes, including CCL21, NRP2, and GJA4. LE2 cells are fundamental components of the lymphatic vasculature, mediating fluid drainage, immune cell trafficking, and participating in tissue homeostasis. Their spatial distribution is predominantly in the skin but can also be enriched in other tissues, such as the submucosal region of the adult colon (Supplementary Table 7).

## Markers

### Core Lymphatic Markers

- **PROX1**: PROX1 is a transcription factor crucial for lymphatic endothelial specification and differentiation. As a key lineage marker, its expression distinguishes LECs from blood endothelial cells and is essential for maintaining lymphatic identity (Supplementary Table 2; T. Adelmann et al., 2020).

- **PDPN (Podoplanin)**: Podoplanin is a transmembrane glycoprotein prominently expressed on LECs but absent in blood vascular endothelium, making it useful for discriminating between these two lineages. Its functional roles include the regulation of lymphatic vessel formation and maintenance of vascular integrity.  
> "On the other hand, LECs expressed both CD31 and Podoplanin (Fig. 1b and Supplementary Fig. 1B)."  
> — Donghyun Paul Jeong et al. (2022)

- **LYVE1**: LYVE1 serves as a cell surface receptor for hyaluronan and is widely recognized as a defining marker for lymphatic endothelium, with a role in leukocyte and fluid transport functions.  
> "Platelet endothelial cell adhesion molecule-1 (PECAM-1 or CD31) is expressed on all cells within the vascular compartment (Fig. 2), whereas the lymphatic vessel endothelial hyaluronan receptor 1 (Lyve-1), a transmembrane glycoprotein that binds hyaluronan, is exclusively on the surface of LECs (Fig. 2)..."  
> — Cynthia Weigel et al. (2023)

### Differentially Expressed Genes (DEGs)

Supplementary analyses further identify signature DEGs for LE2, which likely assist in functional specialization:

- **CCL21**: A chemokine involved in recruiting immune cells to the lymphatic vasculature.
- **NRP2**: Neuropilin-2 participates in lymphangiogenesis and guides cellular responses to VEGF-C.
- **GJA4**: Connexin 37, implicated in gap junction formation and endothelial communication (Supplementary Table 5).

Collectively, the consistent expression of PROX1, PDPN, and LYVE1—alongside select DEGs—reliably identifies LE2 cells in adult skin (Supplementary Table 2; 5; T. Adelmann et al., 2020; William I. Lawrance et al., 2016).

## Location

### Skin

LE2 cells are robustly annotated in the skin, where their presence is confirmed by marker expression and functional studies of human dermal lymphatic endothelial cells (Supplementary Table 7; William I. Lawrance et al., 2016). They are isolated from the dermis and can be found in both healthy and diseased tissue contexts.

### Other Adult Tissues

In spatial enrichment analyses, LE2 cells are also noted to be enriched in the submucosal region of the adult colon (Supplementary Table 7). This underscores their potential functional adaptation in various tissue environments.

## Function

### Fluid Drainage and Barrier Function

LE2 cells, as LECs, mediate unidirectional tissue fluid drainage and sustain tissue fluid homeostasis.  
> "The initial lymphatics are blind-ended vessels that are only capable of unidirectional transport of lymph (5). Lymphatic endothelial cells (LECs) lack mural cells, such as pericytes, with an absent or incomplete basement membrane, and attach themselves to surrounding connective tissue with anchoring filaments..."  
> — Cynthia Weigel et al. (2023)

### Immune Regulation

LE2 cells also play pivotal immunological roles beyond structural support:
> "lymphatic endothelial cells (LECs) not only provide a structural framework for transportation. Recent research has shown that LECs can present antigens and express immunoregulatory molecules that modulate immune cell activation and functions (reviewed in 1,2 )."  
> — Maria H. Ulvmar et al. (2016)

Functional specialization in immune cell recruitment is supported by differential expression of immune-related genes such as CCL21.

### Plasticity and Disease Context

LE2 (LECs) demonstrate phenotype plasticity and context-dependent responses to growth factors as well as pathological stimuli. In fibrotic skin disease, LECs can lose specific markers and transition to myofibroblast-like phenotypes:
> "Lymphatic endothelial cells in intermediate stages of the Ly-EndMT process (i.e., coexpressing LYVE-1 and α-SMA) were found exclusively in the fibrotic skin of SSc patients."  
> — I. Rosa et al. (2023)

This underlines the involvement of LE2 cells in disease progression, particularly fibrosis.

## Structure / Morphology

LE2 cells form part of the initial lymphatic network, characterized by unique architecture:

> "Another structural characteristic unique to the initial lymphatics is the presence of flap valves (Fig. 1D). These valves are formed by overlapping edges of ECs that open to allow influx of interstitial fluid into the initial lymphatics and prevent it from leaking into the interstitium (6)."  
> — Cynthia Weigel et al. (2023)

LE2s typically lack mural cells and reside in thin-walled, blind-ended lymphatic capillaries with specialized anchoring filaments, facilitating tissue fluid absorption (Cynthia Weigel et al., 2023).

Further, primary human dermal LECs—resembling LE2—consistently display authentic expression of Prox-1, LYVE-1, podoplanin, and CD31, but lack blood endothelial markers, confirming their distinct identity (William I. Lawrance et al., 2016).
