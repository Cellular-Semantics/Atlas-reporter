# MHCII+ Macrophages in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Cell Ontology: [tissue-resident macrophage](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000864) (CL:0000864, broad match — NTR: obophenotype/cell-ontology#3612)

## Summary

MHCII+ macrophages are one of four macrophage subsets identified in prenatal human skin (7-17 PCW), alongside LYVE1+, TML (TREM2+ microglia-like), and iron-recycling macrophages (Gopee et al., 2024). The subset was annotated using marker genes established by Suo et al. (2022), who first defined the MHC class II hi macrophage population in a cross-organ atlas of the developing human immune system. MHCII+ macrophages are characterized by high expression of MHC class II genes (HLA-DPA1, HLA-DRA) and the C-type lectin receptor CLEC7A, along with typical macrophage markers F13A1, CD163, and SPP1, while lacking the Langerhans cell markers CD207 and CD1A.

This macrophage subset corresponds to a conserved tissue-resident macrophage population described across multiple organs. Chakarov et al. (2019) identified two distinct interstitial macrophage populations coexisting across tissues: MHCIIhi LYVE1lo macrophages localized to nerves, and LYVE1hi MHCIIlo macrophages localized to blood vessels. Dick et al. (2022) subsequently defined three conserved tissue-resident macrophage subsets across organs -- TLF+ (TIM4/LYVE1/FOLR2-positive), CCR2+, and MHCIIhi (CCR2-negative) -- demonstrating that these populations have distinct origins and life cycles. In the prenatal context, Suo et al. (2022) found that MHCII hi macrophage populations were enriched in later stages of gestation and progressively upregulated immune effector function genes.

## Markers

**Defining markers (atlas-derived):**
- **HLA-DPA1, HLA-DRA**: MHC class II genes whose high expression defines this macrophage subset (Gopee et al., 2024; Suo et al., 2022)
- **CLEC7A**: C-type lectin receptor (Dectin-1), a pattern recognition receptor for fungal beta-glucans that also participates in innate immune signaling (Gopee et al., 2024)

**Shared macrophage markers:**
- **F13A1**: Coagulation factor XIII A chain, expressed by tissue macrophages involved in wound healing and extracellular matrix remodeling (Gopee et al., 2024)
- **CD163**: Hemoglobin-haptoglobin scavenger receptor, a marker of tissue-resident macrophages (Gopee et al., 2024)
- **SPP1**: Osteopontin, a secreted glycoprotein involved in cell adhesion, migration, and immune regulation (Gopee et al., 2024)

**Negative markers:**
- **CD207** (langerin): Absent; distinguishes MHCII+ macrophages from Langerhans cells (Gopee et al., 2024)
- **CD1A**: Absent; further distinguishes from the Langerhans cell lineage (Gopee et al., 2024)

## Location

MHCII+ macrophages reside in the dermis of prenatal human skin. Visium spatial transcriptomics deconvolution predicted co-location of prenatal skin macrophages with endothelial cells in the early and late neurovascular microenvironments (ME1 and ME5) (Gopee et al., 2024).

> "Visium deconvolution analysis predicted co-location of prenatal skin macrophages with endothelial cells ('early and late neurovascular microenvironments', ME1 and ME5)"
>
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

In adult tissues, Chakarov et al. (2019) demonstrated that MHCIIhi macrophages preferentially localize to nerve-associated niches, while LYVE1hi macrophages occupy perivascular niches. This subtissular niche distinction may also apply during prenatal skin development.

## Function

### Angiogenesis

All four macrophage subsets in prenatal skin, including MHCII+ macrophages, express gene programs that drive angiogenesis (Gopee et al., 2024). Macrophage-endothelial cell communication through ligand-receptor interactions (e.g., CXCL8-ACKR1 and CCL8-ACKR1) supports vascular development. Functional validation using skin organoids demonstrated that co-culture with macrophages produced a more elaborate and organized vascular network (Gopee et al., 2024).

> "Gene ontology analysis showed that the four macrophage subsets (LYVE1+, MHCII+, TML and iron-recycling) expressed gene programs that drive angiogenesis"
>
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

### Antigen Presentation and Immune Maturation

The high expression of MHC class II genes indicates a capacity for antigen presentation. Suo et al. (2022) found that acquisition of antigen-presentation properties (MHCII upregulation) by macrophages occurred between 10-12 PCW, coinciding with the expansion of adaptive lymphocytes and the development of lymph nodes. Prior to this, prenatal macrophages expressed primarily pro-angiogenic and tissue morphogenesis signatures.

> "acquisition of macrophage antigen-presentation properties (e.g., MHCII upregulation) between 10 and 12 pcw coincided with the expansion of adaptive lymphocytes"
>
> -- Suo et al. (2022)

Guillaume et al. (2023) demonstrated that MHCII expression on macrophages supports CD4+ T cell homeostasis in the gut, with MHCII expression regulated by both ontogeny and tissue microenvironment. Monocyte-derived macrophages are uniformly MHCIIhi, while prenatally-derived macrophages show more heterogeneous MHCII expression.

### Scarless Healing

Prenatal skin macrophages, including the MHCII+ subset, resemble early macrophages enriched in reindeer antler skin, a model of scarless healing (Gopee et al., 2024). In adult skin, West et al. (2022) showed that the balance between MHCIIhigh and MHCIIintermediate macrophage compartments is critical for tissue homeostasis, with failed restoration of the quiescent macrophage compartment linked to loss of T cell tolerance.
