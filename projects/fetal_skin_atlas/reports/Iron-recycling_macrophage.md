# Iron-Recycling Macrophages in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Cell Ontology: [tissue-resident macrophage](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000864) (CL:0000864, broad match — NTR: obophenotype/cell-ontology#3613)

## Summary

Iron-recycling macrophages are one of four macrophage subsets identified in prenatal human skin by Gopee et al. (2024), alongside LYVE1+, MHCII+, and TREM2+ microglia-like (TML) macrophages. Originally defined as a distinct macrophage grouping across prenatal organs by Suo et al. (2022), this subset is named for its functional specialisation in recycling iron from senescent erythrocytes — a process mediated by the iron exporter ferroportin (SLC40A1). In prenatal skin, iron-recycling macrophages are particularly associated with endothelial cell chemotaxis and angiogenesis. The subset is enriched in later stages of gestation and progressively acquires immune effector functions (Suo et al., 2022).

## Markers

The iron-recycling macrophage annotation was defined in the cross-organ developing human immune system atlas by Suo et al. (2022), who identified eight broad macrophage groupings based on their transcriptome profiles. The full list of differentially expressed genes for this cluster is provided in Supplementary Table 3 of Gopee et al. (2024).

The defining functional marker for this subset is **SLC40A1** (ferroportin), the sole known mammalian cellular iron exporter. Ferroportin is expressed on macrophages, hepatocytes, and enterocytes and is essential for iron egress from cells (Ferreira and Gahl, 2017).

> "SLC40A1 encodes ferroportin, the only known mammalian iron exporter, present in macrophages and in the basolateral membrane of hepatocytes and enterocytes. Loss-of-function mutations lead to classical-or type A-ferroportin disease, characterized by iron accumulation predominantly in macrophages, with increased ferritin but low-to-normal transferrin saturation until late in the disease."
>
> — Ferreira and Gahl (2017)

Iron-recycling macrophages in prenatal skin also express chemokines involved in endothelial cell interactions. Predicted ligand-receptor interactions between macrophages and endothelial cells in prenatal skin include CXCL8-ACKR1 and CCL8-ACKR1 (Gopee et al., 2024).

## Location

### In prenatal skin

Iron-recycling macrophages are present in prenatal human skin from 7 to 17 post-conception weeks (PCW). Visium deconvolution analysis predicted co-location of prenatal skin macrophages, including iron-recycling macrophages, with endothelial cells in neurovascular microenvironments (ME1 and ME5) (Gopee et al., 2024).

### Across prenatal organs

In the cross-organ developing immune atlas, the iron-recycling macrophage grouping was identified across multiple prenatal tissues. Adult erythrophagocytic macrophages from the spleen and liver represent the closest postnatal counterpart to prenatal iron-recycling macrophages (Suo et al., 2022).

> "The majority of adult macrophages clustered separately from the prenatal macrophages, with the exception of erythrophagocytic macrophages (fig. S12, B and C). This population includes macrophages primarily from the spleen and liver that carry out iron-recycling functions"
>
> — Suo et al. (2022)

### Temporal dynamics

Iron-recycling macrophages show temporal enrichment in later gestation, in contrast to LYVE1+ and proliferating macrophages which are enriched in early gestation (Suo et al., 2022).

> "a large fraction of neighborhoods within the ironrecycling and MHCII hi macrophage populations were enriched in later stages of gestation. We found that these subpopulations upregulated genes encoding for immune effector functions"
>
> — Suo et al. (2022)

## Function

### 1. Endothelial cell chemotaxis

Gene module expression analysis in prenatal skin revealed that iron-recycling macrophages are specifically associated with promoting endothelial cell chemotaxis, a distinct functional contribution compared to the other macrophage subsets (Gopee et al., 2024).

> "Gene module expression profiles suggested that sprouting angiogenesis (growth of new vessels) was promoted by LYVE1 + and TML macrophages, blood vessel morphogenesis by LYVE1 + macrophages and endothelial cell chemotaxis by iron-recycling macrophages (Extended Data Fig. 8i and Supplementary Table 27)."
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

### 2. Angiogenesis

All four macrophage subsets in prenatal skin, including iron-recycling macrophages, express gene programs that drive angiogenesis, as shown by gene ontology analysis (Gopee et al., 2024).

> "Gene ontology analysis showed that the four macrophage subsets (LYVE1 + , MHCII + , TML and iron-recycling) expressed gene programs that drive angiogenesis (Supplementary Tables 23-26)."
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

Macrophage-endothelial crosstalk in prenatal skin involves predicted ligand-receptor interactions that support angiogenesis, chemotaxis and cell migration (Gopee et al., 2024).

> "Predicted ligandreceptor interactions were consistent with reciprocal communication between macrophages and endothelial cells to support angiogenesis, chemotaxis and cell migration (for example, CXCL8-ACKR1 and CCL8-ACKR1)"
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

### 3. Iron recycling and tissue iron homeostasis

The core function implied by the name of this subset is erythrophagocytosis and iron recycling — the phagocytosis of senescent or damaged red blood cells, degradation of haemoglobin, and export of iron via ferroportin. This process is fundamental to systemic iron homeostasis and erythropoiesis (Sukhbaatar and Weichhart, 2018; Nairz et al., 2017).

> "Macrophages are sentinel cells of the innate immune system and have important functions in development, tissue homeostasis, and immunity. These phylogenetically ancient cells also developed a variety of mechanisms to control erythropoiesis and the handling of iron. Red pulp macrophages in the spleen, Kupffer cells in the liver, and central nurse macrophages in the bone marrow ensure a coordinated metabolism of iron to support erythropoiesis."
>
> — Sukhbaatar and Weichhart (2018)

At the local tissue level, macrophage-mediated iron redistribution through ferroportin has been shown to be essential for stromal cell proliferation, hair follicle cycling, and wound healing (Recalcati et al., 2018).

> "Iron recycling by macrophages is essential for erythropoiesis, but may also be relevant for iron redistribution to neighboring cells at the local tissue level. Using mice with iron retention in macrophages due to targeted inactivation of the iron exporter ferroportin, we investigated the role of macrophage iron release in hair follicle cycling and wound healing, a complex process leading to major clinical problems, if impaired."
>
> — Recalcati et al. (2018)

This local iron provision function — acting as a tissue "ferrostat" (Winn et al., 2020) — may be particularly relevant in prenatal skin, where iron-recycling macrophages co-locate with endothelial cells and where active erythropoiesis occurs in the developing fetal skin during early gestation.

### 4. Progressive immune effector function

As gestation progresses, iron-recycling macrophages upregulate genes encoding immune effector functions, suggesting a transition from primarily homeostatic iron-handling roles to combined metabolic and immunological functions (Suo et al., 2022). This temporal shift parallels broader changes in prenatal macrophages, which initially appear to serve tissue morphogenesis functions before acquiring traditional immunological capabilities.
