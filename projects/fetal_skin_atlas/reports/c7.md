## Summary

The cluster labeled 'c7' in this adult skin atlas could not be specifically resolved to a defined cell type based on currently available metadata (Name Resolution Evidence, see below). Nevertheless, a wealth of literature focuses on the molecule "C7" (Type VII Collagen, encoded by COL7A1) in the context of skin biology, especially within the dermal-epidermal junction and skin diseases involving C7 deficiency or mutation such as recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (RDEB). C7 is a structural protein produced by both epidermal keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts, serving a crucial role in anchoring fibrils that mediate the strong attachment between the epidermis and the underlying dermis. Key markers include the COL7A1 gene and protein domains such as NC1 and NC2, with localization almost exclusively at the basement membrane zone (BMZ). C7’s absence or dysfunction leads to skin fragility, impaired healing, and fibrotic changes.

## Markers

- **COL7A1 (Type VII Collagen, C7):** The central marker associated with C7 in adult skin, as confirmed by both gene and protein-based detection. Its functional importance is illustrated in several studies:
  
  > "COL7A1 (FC = −2.2), matrilin-2 (MATN2, FC = −1.8), and dermatopontin (DPT, FC = −1.7) were reduced in aged samples but did not reach significance."
  >
  > — McCabe et al. (2020)

- **NC1 and NC2 Domains:** These specialized non-collagenous domains of the C7 protein are widely used for detection and serve as immunological epitopes for diagnostic antibodies.
  
  > "Anti-C7 LH24 monoclonal antibody was used to detect the NC2 domain of C7 (green). Linear green staining at the dermal-epidermal junction indicates the presence of C7, which is absent at baseline but observed at 2 years."
  >
  > — Eichstadt et al. (2019)

  > "B-VEC-mediated COL7A1 gene delivery targets the proper cells and directs the expression of functional, full-length C7 (inclusive of both NC1 and NC2) at the correct location in the dermal-epidermal basement membrane."
  >
  > — Gurevich et al. (2022)

- **Immunofluorescence and Western Blot Markers:** Detection protocols employ polyclonal or monoclonal anti-C7 antibodies for both tissue and cell-based localization and quantification:
  
  > "C7 protein expression in keratinocyte clones was analyzed by western blotting (WB) using an anti-human C7 monospecific polyclonal antibody."
  >
  > — Mencía et al. (2018)

  > "cells were incubated with monospecific polyclonal anti-C7 antibody at 1:5,000 dilution."
  >
  > — Bonafont et al. (2019)

These markers are functionally significant: mutations or reduced abundance of C7 are tightly linked to skin diseases characterized by fragility and loss of epidermal adherence.

## Location

The predominant location for C7 is the basement membrane zone (BMZ) at the dermal-epidermal junction (DEJZ) of the skin.

> "C7 produced by epidermal keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts, is the main component of anchoring fibrils, which are banded structures that secure stable association of the lower side of the lamina densa to the upper papillary dermis of the skin."
>
> — Jover et al. (2024)

### Basement Membrane Zone (BMZ) Localization

- C7 is sharply localized along the BMZ, as confirmed by various immunostaining protocols and functional studies:

  > "Immunofluorescence analysis clearly showed C7 expression in grafts from all clones."
  >
  > — Mencía et al. (2018)
  
  > "Linear green staining at the dermal-epidermal junction indicates the presence of C7, which is absent at baseline but observed at 2 years."
  >
  > — Eichstadt et al. (2019)

  > "Almost continuous deposition of C7 was found along the dermal-epidermal junction in the re-epithelialized area after healing of the treated wound in patient grafts, comparable to staining along the BMZ detected in positive control tissue regenerated from normal human keratinocytes and fibroblasts."
  >
  > — García et al. (2022)

  > "C7 expression within the stratified epidermis was restricted to the basal layer at the basement membrane, consistent with the expression pattern of C7 observed in human skin (Figure 6c)."
  >
  > — du Rand et al. (2024)

### Cell-Type Origin

Evidence supports production by both epidermal keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts:

> "C7 is both a dermal and an epidermal product"
>
> — Thriene et al. (2018)

> "normal human allogenic fibroblasts can also produce detectable C7 and AFs at the BMZ in RDEB skin."
>
> — Bremer et al. (2018)

### Extracutaneous and Graft Models

C7 can be contributed in graft models by endogenous (host) fibroblasts and is highly conserved:

> "murine C7 is deposited in human skin grafts"
>
> — Bremer et al. (2018)

### Developmental, Regenerative, and Disease Contexts

Proper localization is essential; mislocalization seen in disease states leads to functional loss:

> "in healthy skin, C7 is located around the dermoepidermal junction, in the skin of the RDEB patients, the expression of C7 fails to relocate to the sublamina densa, instead spreading across the dermis in the skin."
>
> — Beilin et al. (2021)

## Function

### Structural Integrity and Epidermal-Dermal Adhesion

C7 is essential for the attachment of the epidermis to the dermis via anchoring fibrils, maintaining tissue architecture and mechanical stability.

> "C7 produced by epidermal keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts, is the main component of anchoring fibrils, which are banded structures that secure stable association of the lower side of the lamina densa to the upper papillary dermis of the skin."
>
> — Jover et al. (2024)

> "Laminin-332 and collagen XVII form anchoring filaments that are attached to C7-containing anchoring fibrils (41,42), which provide functional integrity of the dermal-epidermal junction zone (DEJZ) by connecting the collagen IV network of the BM to dermal collagen fibrils (Figure 7) (43-45)."
>
> — Thriene et al. (2018)

### Consequences of Loss or Dysfunction

Absence or faulty secretion and localization of C7 result in skin fragility (blistering), impaired wound healing, chronic inflammation, and progressive fibrosis:

> "The absence of functional C7 is the cause of a profound fragility of the skin and mucous membranes in response to a minor trauma, with constant formation of blisters and erosions 4 ."
>
> — Jover et al. (2024)

> "loss of epidermal C7 leads to a global modification and destabilization of the DEJZ (46)"
>
> — Thriene et al. (2018)

> "Loss of C7 did not only affect the composition of the cellular microenvironment, but it led to global changes in cell homeostasis on mRNA and on protein level."
>
> — Thriene et al. (2018)

### Pro-migratory Matrix Function

Full-length C7 also acts as a matrix that strongly promotes human keratinocyte migration, supporting rapid wound healing:

> "C7 may possess properties that enhance cell migration."
>
> — Woodley et al. (2008)

> "Purified recombinant C7 strongly promoted human keratinocyte migration to levels similar to that generated by type I collagen."
>
> — Woodley et al. (2008)

> "These data indicated that C7 is a potent promotility matrix for human keratinocytes."
>
> — Woodley et al. (2008)

### Immune and Fibrotic Effects

The absence of C7 triggers TGF-β-driven pathways, causing both inflammatory and fibrotic changes that can affect healing and promote carcinogenesis:

> "The pro-inflammatory and pro-fibrotic changes in the absence of C7 lead to an upregulation of TGFβ signaling which drives fibroblast differentiation into myofibroblasts, triggering an increased production of inflammatory cytokines 5 ."
>
> — Jover et al. (2024)

> "loss of C7 leads to far more complex changes as anticipated. Importantly, our study reveals intrinsic epidermal alterations directly caused by C7 deficiency and increased TGF-signaling in epidermal keratinocytes as instigators of dermal fibrosis."
>
> — Thriene et al. (2018)

## Structure / Morphology

C7 forms the main component of "anchoring fibrils"—distinctive, banded ultrastructural elements seen at the BMZ:

> "C7 produced by epidermal keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts, is the main component of anchoring fibrils, which are banded structures that secure stable association of the lower side of the lamina densa to the upper papillary dermis of the skin."
>
> — Jover et al. (2024)

> "this murine C7 is capable of forming AFs that attach to the lamina densa of the human skin graft"
>
> — Bremer et al. (2018)

Abnormalities in anchoring fibrils, such as absence or mislocalization, disrupt this key structural linkage and are hallmarks of C7-associated diseases.
