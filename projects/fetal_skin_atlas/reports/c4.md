# c4
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))  
Scope: adult

## Summary

The cell label "c4" could not be resolved to a conventional cell type name based on the main atlas text or available mapping resources. However, strong evidence from the biomedical literature supports that "C4.4A" is a marker for a population of differentiated keratinocytes within the suprabasal layers of stratified squamous epithelia, especially skin. C4.4A is a glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-anchored membrane protein associated with squamous differentiation, cell adhesion, migration during wound healing, and tumor invasion processes. Its expression is largely restricted to stratified epithelia under physiological conditions and increases under conditions of tissue remodeling or malignancy.

## Markers

### C4.4A

C4.4A emerges as the dominant and specific marker supported by multiple lines of evidence:

> "The GPI-anchored C4.4A glycoprotein was first identified in a highly metastasizing rat pancreatic adenocarcinoma line (Matzku et al, 1989)."  
> — Paret et al. (2007)

> "C4.4A is a marker of squamous differentiation"  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

C4.4A (also known as Ly6/PLAUR domain containing 3) is a GPI-anchored membrane protein and member of the Ly-6 family, which includes proteins involved in cell membrane dynamics, cell-cell adhesion, and signal transduction. Its highly restricted tissue expression makes it a practical marker for identifying differentiated keratinocyte populations in adult skin (Paret et al., 2007; Kriegbaum et al., 2016). Notably, C4.4A is absent or only weakly present in other skin cell types such as melanocytes and nevi and is upregulated in wound healing and various skin tumors (Cheng et al., 2014).

## Location

### Skin and Other Stratified Epithelia

> "In the adult, expression is restricted mostly to stratified epithelial of the skin and squamous epithelia of the upper gastrointestinal tract."  
> — Cheng et al. (2014)

> "Under normal physiological conditions, C4.4A is predominantly expressed in suprabasal layers of stratified squamous epithelia such as those of the skin, hair follicles, esophagus, oral and nasal cavity, vagina and cornea (Figure 1C)."  
> — Jacobsen et al. (2014)

C4.4A is found mainly in the suprabasal layers of stratified squamous epithelia. This includes not only the skin epidermis but also the hair follicles, oral mucosa, esophagus, vagina, and cornea, revealing its functional conservation in various body barriers (Jacobsen et al., 2014; Cheng et al., 2014).

#### Epidermal Substructure

> "The antibody against the C terminus stained the stratum granulosum of healthy human skin."  
> — Paret et al. (2007)

> "A recently described polyclonal hC4.4A-specific antibody that cross-reacts with mC4.4A stained the suprabasal layer of the epidermis (Hansen et al, 2004)."  
> — Paret et al. (2007)

These findings elucidate that in skin, C4.4A localizes predominantly to the cell membranes of suprabasal (above the basal) keratinocyte layers—specifically, the stratum granulosum and stratum spinosum—while being absent from the basal proliferative layer. It is excluded from cell junction structures such as desmosomes (Kriegbaum et al., 2016).

### Developmental and Pathological Context

> "C4.4A expression emerges with the formation of a stratum spinosum layer in an in vitro developing human tissue-engineered skin model."  
> — Jacobsen et al. (2017)

Its emergence in the formation of the stratum spinosum during in vitro development demonstrates a key role in keratinocyte differentiation.

## Function

### Adhesion and Epithelial Structure

> "C4.4A is located at cell-cell contact points between the more differentiated keratinocytes in monolayer culture."  
> — Jacobsen et al. (2017)

The membrane association and spatial localization of C4.4A at regions of cell-cell contact, but its exclusion from desmosomes, supports its role in epithelial organization outside of tight adhesion junctions (Kriegbaum et al., 2016).

### Wound Healing and Cell Migration

> "C4.4A expression was found to become upregulated in migrating keratinocytes during wound healing."  
> — Cheng et al. (2014)

> "After the creation of a wound, this tissue-engineered skin model recapitulates the dynamic expression of C4.4A as seen in the migrating suprabasal murine keratinocytes during wound healing."  
> — Jacobsen et al. (2017)

Both in vivo and in vitro models show C4.4A upregulation during wound healing, specifically in cells at the migratory epithelial front, underscoring its involvement in regeneration and barrier reformation.

> "pronounced expression of C4.4A in keratinocytes and its alleged role in migration in vitro 22,28"  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

> "C4.4A deficiency impacts the kinetics of wound healing in vivo"  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

Experiments in gene-ablated mouse models reveal that absence of C4.4A can delay wound healing, particularly in male mice.

### Tumor Invasion and Prognosis

> "high C4.4A expression has been observed in several types of carcinoma like malignant melanoma, colorectal cancer, breast cancer, urothelial tumor and most pronounced nonsmall cell lung cancer"  
> — Cheng et al. (2014)

> "Seiter (2001) found that normal human skin weakly expresses C4.4A. Melanocytes and nevi are negative, but up to 60% of primary malignant melanoma and 100% of lymph node and skin metastases of melanoma are C4.4A positive."  
> — Cheng et al. (2014)

> "C4.4A is highly expressed in the invasive front of both benign keratoacanthomas and malignant SCCs of the human skin, suggesting a role for C4.4A in the invasive process independent of malignancy 10 ."  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

C4.4A is elevated at the margins of invasive tumors and metastatic foci. While not necessary for normal differentiation, C4.4A expression correlates with tumor cell migration and invasion, making it a potential prognostic marker.


### Dispensability for Basal Homeostasis

> "C4.4A is dispensable for normal squamous epithelial development and differentiation."  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

> "Only few genes were found dysregulated by the C4.4A deficiency per se and all presented rather modest amplitudes, which is congruent with the absence of an overt, manifest pathology in the affected squamous epithelia."  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

Knockout models show C4.4A is not essential for normal skin or mucosal development under steady-state conditions.

## Structure / Morphology

> "C4.4A was found associated to the cell membrane, but it was specifically excluded from the desmosomes bridging the keratinocytes (Fig. 5A)."  
> — Kriegbaum et al. (2016)

C4.4A localizes to the plasma membrane at sites of cell-cell contact, but it does not participate in desmosomal adhesion structures. Its distribution is consistent with a role in lateral membrane interactions of suprabasal keratinocytes.
