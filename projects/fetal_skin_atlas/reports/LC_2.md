# Langerhans Cells

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Langerhans cells (LCs) are specialized, tissue-resident antigen-presenting cells found primarily in the epidermis of adult skin, where they play a crucial role as immune sentinels. In single-cell atlasing of adult skin, "LC_2" corresponds with Langerhans cells, characterized by high expression of canonical markers such as CD207 (Langerin), CD1A, and CD1C. Functionally, LCs sample antigens in the skin barrier environment, orchestrate immune surveillance, and mediate both immunostimulation and immunoregulation. Their unique ontogeny involves local self-renewal under steady-state conditions, but LCs also demonstrate plasticity and migratory capacity in response to inflammation.

## Markers

- **CD207 (Langerin):** A defining marker of LCs, CD207 is a C-type lectin receptor mediating pathogen recognition and the formation of Birbeck granules, which are unique ultrastructural features of LCs. The high expression of CD207 is used to distinguish LCs from other dendritic cell subsets and supports their specialized function in the epidermis (Kim et al., 2014; Bellmann et al., 2020).
- **CD1A:** This MHCI-like molecule is highly expressed on LCs and involved in the presentation of lipid antigens to T cells. Its expression is a characteristic feature, commonly used diagnostically (Zegarska et al., 2017).
- **CD1C:** Identified as a differentially expressed gene in LC_2. It also participates in lipid antigen presentation, further distinguishing LCs from other cutaneous immune cells (Supplementary Findings from the atlas; Deckers et al., 2018).
- **HLA-DR (MHC class II):** Integral for antigen presentation to CD4+ T cells, HLA-DR is highly expressed by LCs and marks their dendritic cell lineage (Zegarska et al., 2017; Boltjes & van Wijk, 2014).
- **CD86:** A costimulatory molecule upregulated during LC maturation, promoting T cell activation (Huang et al., 2006; Oka et al., 2024).
- Additional markers reported include CD11c, SIRPα (CD172a), EpCAM, and E-cadherin, though marker overlap with other myeloid cells necessitates multiparametric analysis for unambiguous identification (Deckers et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2014; Boltjes & van Wijk, 2014).

## Location

### Epidermal Compartment

Langerhans cells form a contiguous, dendritic network in the epidermis, mainly localizing to the suprabasal layers (stratum spinosum) but also present near the basal and stratum corneum layers.

> "Langerhans cells form a contiguous network in the epidermis where they serve as sentinels of skin immunity by capturing and processing antigens that get through the stratum corneum and responding to cell signals activated by surrounding keratinocytes (1)."
>
> — Huang et al. (2006)

> "LC_2 clusters were predominantly localized to the epidermal compartment in adult tissue sections."
>
> — Supplementary Figure 4, atlas

They constitute roughly 2–8% of epidermal cells, forming a network capable of surveying the entire skin surface (Eidsmo & Martini, 2018; Zegarska et al., 2017; Deckers et al., 2018).

> "Langerhans cells (LC) are a unique population of tissue-resident macrophages that form a network of cells across the epidermis of the skin, but which have the ability to migrate from the epidermis to draining lymph nodes (LN)."
>
> — West & Bennett (2018)

> "LCs are distinct subset of DCs that reside in the suprabasal epidermis where they organize a lacelike network (30)."
>
> — Kim et al. (2014)

### Mucosal Tissues

Beyond the skin, LCs are reported in stratified epithelia such as the oral, vaginal, rectal, and oesophageal mucosa, sharing similar functional features but sometimes differing in developmental origin (Gorošová et al., 2008; Deckers et al., 2018).

> "LC were detected in adult skin using TEM and immunohistochemistry, and were found in the epidermis and in the oral, vaginal, rectal and oesophageal mucosa."
>
> — Gorošová et al. (2008)

### Developmental Timing

LCs are seeded from embryonic myeloid progenitors and colonize the epidermis before or around birth, with local self-renewal maintaining the population under steady state. Upon substantial inflammation, monocyte-derived cells can repopulate the epidermal niche (Bellmann et al., 2020; Mass et al., 2023).

## Function

### Immune Sentinels and Antigen Presentation

LCs function as professional antigen-presenting cells and are the first to encounter invading pathogens or noxious agents at the epithelial interface.

> "Langerhans cells, due to their location in the epidermis and their protruding dendrites, are a typical example of a DC sentinel, surveying the epidermis for foreign antigens."
>
> — Boltjes & van Wijk (2014)

They engage in uptake, processing, and presentation of antigens to both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, initiating primary and secondary immune responses.

> "As antigen-presenting cells (APC), LC exhibit an important capacity to initiate primary and secondary immune responses towards foreign proteins encountered at the epithelial interface (epidermis or mucosa) with the environment."
>
> — Hamzeh et al. (2000)

> "LCs constitutively express major histocompatibility complex class I and II (MHCI/MHCII) molecules and present antigens to CD8+ and CD4+ T lymphocytes [75]."
>
> — Cioce et al. (2024)

> "They are able to initiate T H 1, T H 2, T H 17, and T H 22 responses"
>
> — Boltjes & van Wijk (2014)

> "LCs could induce IL-22 production by a subset of CD1a-autoreactive T cells through CD1a molecule to support the epithelial immunologic barrier of the skin (36,37)."
>
> — Kim et al. (2014)

### Immune Regulation and Tolerance

LCs display context-dependent immunoregulatory or immunostimulatory roles depending on environmental cues, inflammation, and maturation state.

> "Studies have delineated the dual roles of LCs as both immunostimulatory and immunoregulatory, dependent on the context and experimental model (Lutz et al, 2010; Stoitzner, 2010; Zhou et al, 2022)."
>
> — Oka et al. (2024)

They can promote T cell tolerance and may modulate homeostasis by interacting with resident T cells, especially regulatory T cells.

> "These findings suggest that LC–T cell interaction in the epidermis may promote T-cell tolerance."
>
> — Oka et al. (2024)

### Migration

Upon activation by environmental signals (e.g., cytokines, danger signals), LCs downregulate adhesion molecules and migrate from the epidermis to draining lymph nodes, where antigen presentation to naïve T cells occurs.

> "Their unique capabilities to self-renew within the epidermis, while also being able to migrate to lymph nodes in order to present antigen, place LCs in a key position to sample the local environment and decide on the appropriate cutaneous immune response."
>
> — Deckers et al. (2018)

## Structure / Morphology

LCs are morphologically distinguished as large, dendritic ("stellate") cells with processes extending between keratinocytes across several epidermal layers.

> "LCs are stellate cells that protrude their dendrites via tight junctions toward the stratum corneum and as such can probe for antigens across several layers of the epidermis without disrupting the permeability barrier."
>
> — Deckers et al. (2018)

A hallmark feature is the presence of Birbeck granules, rod- or tennis racket–shaped organelles seen on electron microscopy and associated with internalized Langerin.

> "LCs have a unique intracellular ultrastructure called Birbeck granule, visible by electron microscope, which is associated with the internalized Langerin (32,33)."
>
> — Kim et al. (2014)

> "They are recognized through the expression of CD1a antigen and specific organelles: the Birbeck granules."
>
> — Hamzeh et al. (2000)
