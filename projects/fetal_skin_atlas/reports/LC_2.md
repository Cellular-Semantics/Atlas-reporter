# Regulatory Langerhans Cell (LC_2) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [Langerhans cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000453) (CL:0000453, broad match — no exact CL term for regulatory LC substate)

## Summary

LC_2 is a Langerhans cell (LC) subset in adult human epidermis defined by Reynolds et al. (2021) in their PAGA-based differentiation analysis of skin antigen-presenting cells. In the framework established by Liu et al. (2021) — cited in Reynolds et al. and reviewed by Vine et al. (2022) — LC2 is a novel steady-state LC subset distinct from classic LC1: it expresses lower Langerin (CD207) and higher CD1c and CD1b, placing it phenotypically between classic LCs and dermal dendritic cells. Functional evidence suggests LC2 acts as a "regulatory LC" with a primary immunomodulatory role under steady-state and inflammatory conditions. LC2 appears to be enriched in foreskin epidermis and may be less abundant or absent in trunk skin (Tamminga et al., 2025, citing Liu et al., 2021).

## Markers

> "Recent single cell transcriptomic analysis of the human stratified squamous epithelium has suggested that LCs exist as multiple subsets; two at steady state termed LC1 and LC2, an activated subset defined by high CD83 and low CCR7 expression termed LC3, and a migratory subset defined by high CCR7 expression termed LC4"
>
> — Vine et al. (2022), reviewing Liu et al. (2021)

> "Of the two steadystate LCs, LC1 were delineated as classic LCs while LC2 were described as a novel and unique subset of LC"
>
> — Vine et al. (2022)

Key markers of LC2:
- **CD207 (Langerin)** — low expression (distinguishes LC2 from LC1)
- **CD1c** — high expression; typically a marker of dermal DCs rather than classical LCs
- **CD1b** — high expression
- **CLEC10A (MGL)** — top differentially expressed gene in LC2 (Tamminga et al., 2025)
- **LYZ (lysozyme)** — top differentially expressed gene in LC2

## Location

LC2 cells reside within the epidermis, positioned alongside LC1 as the two steady-state LC subsets. Evidence from Tamminga et al. (2025) and Liu et al. (2021) suggests LC2 abundance varies by body site, with greater representation in foreskin than in trunk skin. Reynolds et al. (2021) include LC2 within the epidermal LC compartment characterised by PAGA analysis.

## Function

### 1. Immunoregulation and tolerance

LC2 is proposed to function primarily as a "regulatory LC" that modulates adaptive immune responses and promotes tolerance. As reviewed by Vine et al. (2022):

> "Of the two steadystate LCs, LC1 were delineated as classic LCs while LC2 were described as a novel and unique subset of LC"
>
> — Vine et al. (2022)

Its high expression of CLEC10A (MGL/CD301), a C-type lectin with known immunosuppressive signalling properties, and CD1c (a non-classical antigen-presenting receptor for lipid antigens) supports an immunomodulatory role distinct from the classical antigen-presentation function of LC1.

### 2. Relationship to LC differentiation trajectory

Reynolds et al. (2021) show by PAGA analysis that the four LC clusters form a differentiation trajectory leading from steady-state LC1/LC2 through activated LC3 to migratory LC4, which converges with the migratory DC signature. LC2 occupies a second steady-state branch of this trajectory, from which cells activate and acquire the CCR7-high migratory programme.

### 3. Disease associations

Tamminga et al. (2025) report enrichment of migratory LCs and LC2 in AD lesional skin compared with healthy skin. LC2 proportional expansion in inflammatory skin disease may reflect the skin's attempt to modulate the immune response through regulatory mechanisms.

## Disease relevance

LC2 is enriched in AD lesional skin (Tamminga et al., 2025). Its expression of DC-associated markers (CD1c, CD1b) has generated debate about whether LC2 represents a bona fide LC subset or a recently defined epidermal/epithelial DC population — an unresolved classification question with implications for understanding skin immune responses in disease.
