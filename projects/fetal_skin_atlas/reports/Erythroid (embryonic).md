# Erythroid (Embryonic) in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Cell Ontology: [erythroblast](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000765) (CL:0000765, broad match -- no CL term for embryonic/primitive-specific erythroblast)

## Summary

Erythroid (embryonic) cells are a more mature population of primitive erythroid cells identified in the prenatal human skin atlas (Gopee et al., 2024), distinguished from the "Early erythroid (embryonic)" progenitors by their more advanced differentiation state. These cells represent erythroblasts of the primitive wave, originating from yolk sac-derived progenitors, and are characterized by expression of embryonic hemoglobin genes (HBZ, HBE1). Popescu et al. (2019) mapped the temporal shift from embryonic to fetal hemoglobin subunit expression across developing haematopoietic tissues, confirming that yolk sac-derived erythroblasts express Gower 1 and 2 hemoglobins. In the 7-17 PCW window of the atlas, embryonic erythroid cells represent a declining population as definitive (fetal) erythropoiesis becomes dominant.

## Markers

- **HBZ** (zeta-globin): Embryonic alpha-like globin, component of Hb Gower 1 and Hb Portland.
- **HBE1** (epsilon-globin): Embryonic beta-like globin, component of Hb Gower 1 and Hb Gower 2.

> "Erythroblasts show expression of haemoglobin genes, and a temporal shift from Gower 1 and 2 subunit expression (HBZ, HBE1) to fetal haemoglobin subunit expression (HBA1 and HBG2) between yolk sac and liver."
>
> -- Popescu et al. (2019)

Embryonic erythroid cells are distinguished from fetal erythroid cells by their globin expression profile: embryonic hemoglobins Gower 1 (zeta2-epsilon2), Gower 2 (alpha2-epsilon2), and Portland (zeta2-gamma2), versus fetal hemoglobin (alpha2-gamma2).

## Location

### Prenatal skin

Erythroid (embryonic) cells are annotated in the prenatal skin atlas between 7-17 PCW (Gopee et al., 2024). These circulating primitive erythroid cells are present in the developing vasculature throughout the embryo and fetus during early gestation.

### Yolk sac and circulation

Primitive erythropoiesis initiates in the yolk sac, producing a transient cohort of nucleated erythroblasts that enter the circulation. In humans, embryonic hemoglobin is present from approximately 4 weeks and persists until 10-12 weeks of gestation, overlapping with the early portion of the atlas window (Popescu et al., 2019).

## Function

### Oxygen transport during early development

Embryonic erythroid cells produce hemoglobins (Gower 1, Gower 2, Portland) with oxygen-binding properties adapted to early embryonic development, before the full establishment of placental oxygen exchange. These embryonic hemoglobins are progressively replaced by fetal hemoglobin (HbF) as definitive erythropoiesis takes over.

### Primitive erythropoiesis

Unlike definitive erythroblasts, primitive erythroid cells characteristically circulate as large nucleated cells. They undergo enucleation later in development and represent an evolutionarily conserved mechanism for producing the first functional red blood cells in the embryo.

## Structure / Morphology

Primitive erythroid cells are morphologically distinct from definitive erythrocytes. They are large, nucleated erythroblasts that progressively mature in the bloodstream. In mouse embryos, they progress from immature proerythroblasts to basophilic and ultimately orthochromatic erythroblasts, before late-stage enucleation. Human primitive erythroblasts follow a similar maturation sequence.
