# Monocyte-Derived Dendritic Cell Subtype 1 (moDC_1) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [monocyte-derived dendritic cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0011031) (CL:0011031, broad match)

## Summary

The moDC_1 cluster represents the least mature subtype of monocyte-derived dendritic cells in adult human skin, as defined by Reynolds et al. (2021). MoDCs constitute one of three mononuclear phagocyte differentiation branches identified by PAGA analysis, alongside Langerhans cells and myeloid DCs (DC1/DC2). moDC_1 retains subset-identity genes and represents the earliest state in the moDC maturation trajectory, progressing through moDC_2 toward the terminally mature moDC_3 state. Skin moDCs are derived from blood monocytes that infiltrate tissue and differentiate into dendritic cells, a process driven by inflammatory signals and homeostatic cues. The adult skin annotations including moDC subtypes were integrated into the Gopee et al. (2024) prenatal skin atlas for cross-developmental comparisons.

## Markers

### Monocyte-derived lineage markers

MoDCs in human skin are part of the broader mononuclear phagocyte compartment. Reynolds et al. (2021) identified 14 states of mononuclear phagocytes in human skin and annotated them by alignment with blood mononuclear phagocytes. In the epidermis, moDCs are characterized as:

> "the epidermal HLA-DR+CD1a-Lang-CD11c+CD1c+cells are predominantly moDCs and correspond with non-LC like epidermal cells potent at stimulating T cell proliferation, pro-inflammatory cytokine production and transmission of HIV to CD4+T cells"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

moDC_1 retains subset-identity genes that are progressively lost during maturation toward the moDC_3 state.

### General moDC features

Monocyte-derived dendritic cells arise from circulating monocytes (CD14+ in humans) that infiltrate tissues and differentiate under inflammatory signals including GM-CSF, IL-4, and TNF-alpha (Mazzoccoli and Liu, 2024). Human moDCs express CD14, CD11c, CD64, and CD88, and exhibit robust antigen-presenting capabilities (Pachocki et al., 2025).

## Location

### Dermis and epidermis of adult skin

MoDCs are present in both the dermis and epidermis of healthy adult skin. Reynolds et al. (2021) demonstrated that epidermal moDCs are HLA-DR+CD1a-CD11c+CD1c+ and are enriched within the Langerin-negative epidermal compartment. In the dermis, moDCs overlap phenotypically with CD14+ cells previously characterized by McGovern et al. (2014), who established that human dermal CD14+ cells are:

> "a tissue-resident population of monocyte-derived macrophages with a short half-life of <6 days"
>
> — McGovern et al. (2014)

The distinction between moDC and monocyte-derived macrophages reflects the complexity of the monocyte differentiation landscape in human skin.

## Function

### Antigen presentation and T cell stimulation

moDC_1, as a less mature moDC state, is likely involved in antigen uptake and early processing in the tissue microenvironment. General moDC functions include cross-presentation of exogenous antigens on MHC class I to CD8+ T cells and programming CD4+ T cells toward Th1, Th2, and Th17 phenotypes (Mazzoccoli and Liu, 2024). Epidermal moDCs are described as potent stimulators of T cell proliferation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production (Reynolds et al., 2021).

### Position in differentiation trajectory

PAGA analysis in Reynolds et al. (2021) revealed that moDC_1 sits at the beginning of the monocyte-derived DC differentiation branch. As cells progress from moDC_1 through moDC_2 to moDC_3, they acquire maturation markers (CD83, CCR7, LAMP3) and migration genes (FSCN1) while losing subset-identity markers. This trajectory reflects the transition from tissue-resident antigen-sampling cells to mature migratory dendritic cells capable of priming T cells in draining lymph nodes.
