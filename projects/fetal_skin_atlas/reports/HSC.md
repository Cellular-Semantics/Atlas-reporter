# Haematopoietic Stem Cells (HSC) in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Cell Ontology: [hematopoietic stem cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000037) (CL:0000037, broad match)

## Summary

Haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) are identified as a distinct cell population in the prenatal human skin atlas assembled by Gopee et al. (2024), spanning 7-17 post-conception weeks (PCW). HSCs are multipotent cells from which all lymphoid and myeloid lineages develop. Their presence in fetal skin was independently confirmed by Suo et al. (2022), who demonstrated that haematopoiesis is not restricted to the developing liver and bone marrow during this gestational window. In the prenatal skin atlas, HSCs sit within the broader haematopoietic progenitor (Haem progenitor) compartment, alongside megakaryocyte-erythroid-mast cell progenitors (MEMPs), erythroid progenitors, and lymphoid progenitors. Popescu et al. (2019) established the developmental hierarchy of human fetal liver haematopoiesis, identifying a central HSC/MPP node from which erythroid-megakaryocyte-mast, B cell, innate/T-lymphoid, and myeloid lineages radiate.

## Markers

No specific marker gene lists for HSCs in prenatal skin were provided in the atlas supplementary materials. However, the haematopoietic hierarchy described by Popescu et al. (2019) identifies key features of fetal HSC/MPPs:

> "By force directed graph (FDG), we identify three connections to a central HSC/MPP node featuring erythroid-megakaryocyte-mast cells, B cell and innate/T-lymphoid cells and myeloid cells."
>
> -- Popescu et al. (2019)

In the fetal liver, the HSC/MPP population at its base expresses CLEC9A, HLA-DRA, and high levels of primitive genes including MLLT3, consistent with a multipotent long-term repopulating HSC (Popescu et al., 2019). Human fetal HSCs are typically isolated as CD34-positive, CD38-negative, CD45RA-negative, CD90-positive cells (Popescu et al., 2019).

## Location

### Prenatal skin

HSCs are explicitly annotated in the prenatal skin atlas as part of the immune/haematopoietic compartment (Gopee et al., 2024). Their presence in developing skin was questioned by peer reviewers as possible contamination from fetal liver or bone marrow, but the authors confirmed sample integrity and the finding is consistent with independent observations.

> "we detected B cell progenitors in almost all prenatal organs, megakaryocyte/erythroid progenitors in developing spleen and skin, and myeloid progenitors in the thymus, spleen, skin, and kidney"
>
> -- Suo et al. (2022)

> "This finding suggests that hematopoiesis is not restricted to developing liver and bone marrow between 7-17 pcw and other organs can also support blood and immune cell differentiation during prenatal development."
>
> -- Suo et al. (2022)

### Fetal liver

The primary site of HSC expansion and differentiation during the 7-17 PCW window is the fetal liver (Popescu et al., 2019). Definitive haematopoiesis in the fetal liver supports self-renewal and differentiation of HSC/MPPs into all blood and immune lineages.

## Function

### Multipotent self-renewal and differentiation

HSCs are defined by their capacity for self-renewal and multilineage differentiation. In the fetal liver context, Popescu et al. (2019) demonstrated that single-cell culture of CD34-positive, CD38-negative, CD45RA-negative HSC/MPPs yields both unilineage and multipotential colonies containing erythroid, myeloid, NK, and B cell progeny. There is a significant reduction in trilineage colonies with gestational age, and colonies containing erythroid cells decrease while those containing NK cells and B cells increase with gestational age (Popescu et al., 2019).

### Support of extramedullary haematopoiesis in skin

The presence of HSCs in prenatal skin, alongside downstream progenitors (MEMP, erythroid progenitors, megakaryocytes, B cell progenitors), suggests that fetal skin can support limited extramedullary haematopoiesis during development (Suo et al., 2022). This may reflect the developing vasculature serving as a niche for circulating progenitors or active local haematopoiesis.

## Structure / Morphology

No specific morphological descriptions of skin-resident fetal HSCs were found in the traversed literature.
