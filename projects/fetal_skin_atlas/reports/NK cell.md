# Natural Killer Cells in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))  
Scope: fetal  
Cell Ontology: [natural killer cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000623) (CL:0000623, exact match)

## Summary

Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphoid cells identified in prenatal human skin (7-17 PCW) as a distinct immune cluster in the single-cell atlas of Gopee et al. (2024). In fetal skin, NK cells are among the most abundant immune cell types after macrophages and are detectable from as early as 8 PCW (Botting & Haniffa, 2020; Popescu et al., 2019). They are defined by high expression of KLRF1, NKG7, GZMB, GNLY, and KLRD1, and express genes for perforin, killer cell lectin receptors (KLRs), interferon-gamma, and multiple granzymes, showing resemblance to functionally mature NK cells (Supplementary Table S3; Botting & Haniffa, 2020). However, fetal NK cells are developmentally distinct from their adult counterparts: they lack the most mature phenotype (CD94-CD16+) found in adult blood, show stereotyped Eomes-high/T-bet-low expression in immature stages, and exhibit selective hyporesponsiveness to MHC class I-negative targets that may contribute to fetal-maternal tolerance (Collins et al., 2017; Vermijlen & Prinz, 2014; Ivarsson et al., 2013). In the prenatal skin atlas, NK cells co-locate with dendritic and other lymphoid cells in pre-dermal condensate microenvironments, suggesting a potential role in the immune niche supporting early hair follicle formation.

## Markers

Key marker genes for fetal skin NK cells, as established by differential expression analysis in the prenatal skin atlas (Supplementary Table S3):

- **KLRF1 (Killer cell lectin-like receptor subfamily F, member 1):** Strongly expressed in the NK cell cluster; a marker of mature cytotoxic NK cells involved in target recognition and activation.

- **NKG7 (Natural Killer Cell Granule Protein 7):** An effector molecule associated with cytotoxic granule release and degranulation, crucial for direct cell killing.

- **GZMB (Granzyme B):** A serine protease delivered to target cells to induce apoptosis; central to NK cell cytotoxicity.

- **GNLY (Granulysin):** A cytotoxic granule protein with antimicrobial and tumoricidal effects.

- **KLRD1 (CD94):** Important for NK cell activation and target cell recognition; forms heterodimers with NKG2 family members to regulate cytotoxic activity.

### Fetal-Specific Marker Features

Fetal NK cells exhibit distinct marker profiles compared to adult NK cells:

> "These NK cells expressed genes for perforin, killer cell lectin receptors (KLRs), interferon-c and multiple granzymes, showing resemblance to functionally mature NK cells."
>
> — Botting & Haniffa (2020)

> "The most mature NK cells found in adult (or postnatal) human circulation (CD94-CD16+) are absent during ontogeny."
>
> — Collins et al. (2017)

Fetal NK cells display a stereotyped pattern of Eomes and T-bet transcription factor expression during development:

> "Human fetal NK cells were found to express the 2 signature T-box transcription factors essential for the development of all murine NK and NK-like cells, eomesodermin (Eomes) and T-bet. The single-cell pattern of Eomes and T-bet expression during ontogeny, however, revealed a stereotyped pattern of reciprocal dominance, with immature NK cells expressing higher amounts of Eomes and more mature NK cells marked by greater abundance of T-bet."
>
> — Collins et al. (2017)

An unexpected feature of fetal NK cells is the expression of cytoplasmic CD3 components absent in adult NK cells:

> "Unlike adult NK cells, freshly isolated fetal liver NK cells and clones derived from these cells, as well as a subset of cord blood NK cells, express substantial levels of CD3 delta and CD3 epsilon proteins in the cytoplasm."
>
> — Phillips et al. (1992)

## Location

### In Prenatal Skin

NK cells are identified as a distinct cluster within the immune compartment of prenatal human skin from 7-17 PCW (Gopee et al., 2024, Fig. 1e). Spatial microenvironment analysis revealed that pre-dermal condensate cells co-located with dendritic and lymphoid cells, including NK cells, in correlation analyses (Fig. 1d, Extended Data Fig. 1e,f). NK cells are enriched in the stromal region in adult skin (Supplementary Table S6).

> "Natural killer (NK) cells and innate lymphoid cell (ILC) precursors have been identified in skin as early as 8 PCW. Despite being the most abundant immune cell types isolated from fetal skin after macrophages, these are otherwise poorly described."
>
> — Botting & Haniffa (2020)

> "Single cell transcriptomic profiling of developing fetal and adult healthy human skin compared with skin from adult patients with atopic dermatitis and psoriasis demonstrated an enrichment of ILCs within the skin during the first trimester for fetal tissue."
>
> — Johnson & Lee (2025)

### In Other Fetal Tissues

Ivarsson et al. (2013) phenotypically and functionally compared NK cells across fetal lung, spleen, liver, bone marrow, and mesenteric lymph nodes, finding that NK cells in fetal lung were more differentiated than in other sites. Since fetal skin, like lung, is exposed to amniotic fluid, fetal skin NK cells may share a similar differentiated profile (Botting & Haniffa, 2020).

NK cell-restricted progenitors have been identified in fetal liver from gestational week 6 and in fetal bone marrow from week 12 (Hernandez-Torres & Stehle, 2022). Between 18-36 PCW, NK cells are found across fetal liver, lung, and spleen with organ-specific variations in KIR expression.

> "We also observed a stereotyped pattern of tissue-specific NK cell maturation during human ontogeny, with fetal liver being more restrictive to NK cell maturity than fetal bone marrow, spleen, or lung."
>
> — Collins et al. (2017)

### Developmental Origin

NK cells are among the earliest innate lymphoid cells to arise during fetal development. They can derive from multiple waves of hematopoiesis:

> "Natural killer cells are the founding member of the innate lymphoid cell (ILC) family and are generally grouped based on their organ of development and tissue localization: we distinguish bone marrow-derived or adult conventional NK (cNK) cells, thymic-derived, fetal liver-derived, liver-resident, uterine-resident, and intestinal-resident NK cells."
>
> — Carotta (2016)

> "Fetal EMP-derived NK cells are highly potent cytotoxic cells, yet hyporesponsive to HLA-negative target cells compared to their adult HSC-derived counterparts."
>
> — Feyaerts et al. (2022)

## Function

### Cytotoxicity in the Fetus

Fetal NK cells are capable of cytolytic function, but with important distinctions from adult NK cells:

> "We demonstrate that functional NK cells exist in liver at early stages of human embryonic development. Freshly isolated fetal NK cells mediated MHC-unrestricted cytotoxicity against NK-sensitive targets and acquired the ability to lyse NK-resistant tumors after overnight culture in interleukin 2."
>
> — Phillips et al. (1992)

> "Human fetal NK cells differentiate early in utero and are highly responsive to cytokines and antibody-mediated stimulation but respond poorly to MHC class I-negative target cells."
>
> — Vermijlen & Prinz (2014)

### Fetal-Maternal Tolerance

A key functional distinction of fetal NK cells is their selective hyporesponsiveness, which may contribute to immune tolerance during gestation:

> "In contrast to adult NK cells, it appears that expression of KIRs did not educate fetal NK cells but rendered them hyporesponsive to target cells lacking MHC class I. This selective hyporesponsiveness could contribute to fetal-maternal tolerance in utero."
>
> — Vermijlen & Prinz (2014)

> "Thus, human NK cells can be made early during gestation, but they appear to be functionally different than adult NK cells."
>
> — Vermijlen & Prinz (2014)

### Regulation of Cutaneous Microbiota Colonization

In mouse models, NK1.1+ ILC1 cells in fetal and neonatal skin play a role in regulating early commensal microbiota colonization, a function potentially relevant to human fetal skin NK cells:

> "In mice, NK 1.1+ ILC1 cells are highly enriched in fetal and neonatal skin, but rapidly decline within 6 weeks of life. These cells are believed to play a significant role in regulating early cutaneous microbiota colonization."
>
> — Johnson & Lee (2025)

> "NK1.1+ ILC1s generated in fetal thymi are also programmed with preferential acquisition of the CCR10+ skin-homing property. Correspondingly, most skin ILCs in neonatal mice are NK1.1+ ILC1s, which play an important role in regulation of the skin colonization of commensal bacteria."
>
> — Yang et al. (2020)

### Potential Role in Skin Morphogenesis

The prenatal skin atlas revealed that immune cells occupy defined microanatomical niches where they may have non-immune functions during early gestation. NK cells co-locate with dendritic and other lymphoid cells in microenvironments associated with pre-dermal condensate cells (Gopee et al., 2024), suggesting potential contributions to the immune niche supporting hair follicle formation. The atlas further demonstrated that innate immune cells are key players in skin morphogenesis beyond conventional immunity, achieved through crosstalk with non-immune cells.

## Structure / Morphology

NK cells are morphologically characterized as large granular lymphocytes containing cytoplasmic granules that store perforin, granzymes, and other effector molecules:

> "Natural killer (NK) cells are large granular lymphocytes of the innate immune system that participate in the early control of microbial infections and cancer."
>
> — Gregoire et al. (2007)

Fetal NK cells possess unique structural features, notably the cytoplasmic expression of CD3 delta and CD3 epsilon proteins that are absent from adult NK cells (Phillips et al., 1992). This finding suggests that fetal NK cells may represent a distinct subpopulation predominant in the fetus, or alternatively, that NK and T cells arise from a common progenitor expressing CD3 complex components.
