# Activated Langerhans Cell (LC_3) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [Langerhans cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000453) (CL:0000453, broad match — no exact CL term for activated LC substate)

## Summary

LC_3 is an activated Langerhans cell (LC) subset in adult human epidermis. In the four-subset LC framework established by Liu et al. (2021) and reviewed by Vine et al. (2022), LC3 is defined by high CD83 expression and low CCR7 expression — distinguishing it from the migratory LC4 state (which has high CCR7) and from the steady-state LC1/LC2 subsets. Reynolds et al. (2021) describe the LC differentiation trajectory by PAGA analysis, in which LC3 represents a maturation intermediate: it has acquired DC maturation markers (CD83, CD40, CD86, LAMP3) and immunoregulatory markers (CD274/PD-L1, IDO1) without yet fully committing to the CCR7-high migratory programme. LC3 thus marks the transition between epidermal steady-state and lymph-node-directed migration.

## Markers

> "Recent single cell transcriptomic analysis of the human stratified squamous epithelium has suggested that LCs exist as multiple subsets; two at steady state termed LC1 and LC2, an activated subset defined by high CD83 and low CCR7 expression termed LC3, and a migratory subset defined by high CCR7 expression termed LC4"
>
> — Vine et al. (2022), reviewing Liu et al. (2021)

Key markers of LC3:
- **CD83** — high expression; maturation and activation marker for DCs/LCs
- **CCR7** — low expression (distinguishes LC3 from LC4)
- **CD40** — co-stimulatory; maturation marker
- **CD86** — co-stimulatory; maturation marker
- **LAMP3** — lysosomal/maturation marker; part of the shared DC maturation signature

Reynolds et al. (2021) describe the shared maturation signature at the convergence of LC, myeloid DC, and monocyte-derived DC branches:

> "The clusters at the convergence of these branches (moDC3, LC4 and migratory DC (Mig. DC)) express transcripts associated with DC maturation (CD83, CCR7, LAMP3, CD40, CD86), immunoregulation (CD274, IDO1, CD200, PDCD1LG2, SOCS1) and migration (FSCN1, PLGRKT, TRAF1, BCL2A1, CFLAR and REL)"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

Note: in the Reynolds nomenclature, the full convergence signature with high CCR7 and migration genes corresponds to LC4/Mig. DC; LC3 represents the pre-migratory activated intermediate with high CD83 but low CCR7.

## Location

LC3 cells are activated LCs undergoing maturation within or near the epidermis of adult skin. As an intermediate state on the LC differentiation trajectory, LC3 cells are likely transitioning from the epidermal LC niche toward the dermis and lymphatic vessels as they acquire the migratory programme fully (LC4 state).

## Function

### 1. Homeostatic LC maturation

LC3 represents the activated/maturation intermediate in the LC differentiation trajectory. Reynolds et al. (2021) use PAGA analysis to show that the LC branch converges toward a common DC maturation programme:

> "Partition-based approximate graph abstraction (PAGA) analysis revealed three branches of differentiation: LCs, myeloid DCs (DC1 and DC2) and monocyte-derived DCs (mo-DCs)"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

In this topology, LC3 sits between the steady-state LC1/LC2 states and the fully migratory LC4 state. Homeostatic LC maturation — even in the absence of overt inflammation — continuously generates activated/migratory LCs to maintain immune surveillance of skin draining lymph nodes.

### 2. Antigen presentation and T cell priming

Activated LCs upregulate co-stimulatory molecules (CD40, CD86) and maturation markers (CD83, LAMP3), equipping them to prime naïve T cells upon arrival at skin-draining lymph nodes. The immunoregulatory gene expression also present in this state (CD274/PD-L1, IDO1, SOCS1) suggests that LC3 may simultaneously prime and regulate T cell responses, contributing to peripheral tolerance.

### 3. Conserved maturation programme across DC lineages

Reynolds et al. (2021) show the LC maturation gene signature is shared across DC lineages and conserved in disease:

> "Surprisingly, the migratory DC signature is also present across disease states including tonsillitis, ascites, lung cancer and rheumatoid arthritis"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

This conservation suggests that the LC3 activation programme represents a general DC homeostatic maturation pathway rather than a skin-specific process.

## Disease relevance

The activated/maturation LC state (LC3) is part of the homeostatic DC maturation continuum that is augmented in inflammatory skin disease. Reynolds et al. (2021) previously reported migratory gene expression in fetal thymic medullary DCs, suggesting these developmental LC programmes are utilised in adult tissue homeostasis and amplified in disease.
