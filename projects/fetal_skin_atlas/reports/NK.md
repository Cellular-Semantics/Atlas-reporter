# Natural Killer (NK) Cells
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Natural Killer (NK) cells, or Natural Killer cells as consistently resolved in adult human skin, are a specialized subset of lymphoid-derived innate immune lymphocytes recognized for bridging innate and adaptive immunity (Name Resolution; Johnson et al., 2025). In single-cell transcriptomic analyses of human skin, adult NK cells are distinguished by high expression of the marker genes KLRF1, NKG7, GZMB, GNLY, and KLRD1, and are notably enriched in the stromal region of the tissue (Supplementary Findings). Functionally, NK cells are cytotoxic effectors capable of direct target cell killing, cytokine production, and immunoregulation, with context-dependent alterations in disease or inflammation. Evidence from recent studies supports their diverse marker profile, spatial localization in skin, and their role in both steady-state immune surveillance and response to pathology.

## Markers

Key marker genes for adult skin NK cells, as established by differential expression analysis, include:

- **KLRF1 (Killer cell lectin-like receptor subfamily F, member 1):** Strongly expressed in NK cell clusters and commonly used as a marker of mature cytotoxic NK cells; plays a role in target recognition and activation (Supplementary Table S3; Johnson et al., 2025).
- **NKG7 (Natural Killer Cell Granule Protein 7):** An effector molecule associated with cytotoxic granule release and degranulation—crucial for direct cell killing (Supplementary Table S3).
- **GZMB (Granzyme B):** A serine protease delivered to target cells to induce apoptosis, central to NK cell cytotoxicity (Supplementary Table S3).
- **GNLY (Granulysin):** Another cytotoxic granule protein with antimicrobial and tumoricidal effects (Supplementary Table S3).
- **KLRD1 (CD94):** Important for NK cell activation and target cell recognition, forms heterodimers involved in regulating cytotoxic activity (Supplementary Table S3).

The high expression of KLRF1, NKG7, and GZMB formed the primary basis for cluster annotation as NK cells in atlas data (Supplementary Table S3).

In addition to these, literature evidence supports other classical NK cell surface markers and notes their importance in functional and developmental contexts:

> "The expression of the CD56 (neural cell adhesion molecule (NCAM)) surface marker is indicative of the acquisition of distinct functional properties by human mature NK cells"
>
> — Portale et al. (2023)

> "Subtypes of human NK cells have traditionally been described on the basis of their relative expression of CD16 and CD56 cell surface antigens"
>
> — Lee et al. (2019)

There is also context-dependent marker expression, including tissue-resident markers:

> "A proportion of them express molecules known as tissue-resident markers for T cells, such as CD69 and CD103"
>
> — Huot et al. (2020)

## Location

### In Human Skin

The atlas data show that NK cells are enriched in the stromal region of adult human skin (Supplementary Table S6). This pattern is corroborated by multiple studies, suggesting that NK cells are present at variable frequencies in many tissues, including the skin:

> "NK cells are frequent in organs such as the liver and the uterus but are probably present in all tissues to various degrees, including bone marrow, lymph nodes, spleen, kidneys, skin, lung, and gut"
>
> — Huot et al. (2020)

Spatial localization may change in disease states or following infection, with studies reporting:

> "we demonstrated increased dermal expression of both NK cell markers NCAM-1/CD56 and pan-granzyme, corroborating the skin-homing, mostly in severe AD."
>
> — de Lima et al. (2024)

### Developmental Context

NK cell ontogeny originates from bone marrow precursors, with subsequent migration to blood and tissues:

> "NK cell precursors originate from the bone marrow and go through a complex maturation process that leads to the acquisition of their effector functions...to their redistribution from the bone marrow and lymph nodes to blood, spleen, liver, and lung."
>
> — Grégoire et al. (2007)

> "NK cells comprise 5-10% of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), but they are also found with a variable frequency in various lymphoid and non-lymphoid tissues including BM, liver, lung, skin, kidney and spleen"
>
> — Seliger et al. (2022)

During acute inflammation or infection:

> "Responding NK cells have a less mature phenotype and a distinct chemokine-receptor imprint indicative of skin-homing."
>
> — Zimmer et al. (2019)

> "A corresponding NK cell subset can be localized to skin early during acute infection."
>
> — Zimmer et al. (2019)

## Function

### Cytotoxicity and Host Defense

NK cells are potent cytotoxic effector lymphocytes crucial for the elimination of infected, stressed, or transformed cells:

> "Natural killer (NK) cells are lymphoid-derived cells that play a critical role in bridging innate and adaptive immunity."
>
> — Johnson et al. (2025)

> "Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphocytes endowed with potent killing abilities against foreign, stressed, infected, and transformed cells that may be dangerous to the host."
>
> — Portale et al. (2023)

> "Natural killer (NK) cells are cytotoxic innate immune cells that were first described in 1973 by E. Klein and colleagues...Based on their cytolytic function, NK cells play a key role in the first line of immune defense and are able to directly eliminate tumor or pathogen-infected cells."
>
> — Seliger et al. (2022)

### Cytokine Production and Immunoregulation

NK cells shape immune responses by producing and secreting diverse cytokines and chemokines:

> "NK cells can also produce cytokines with antiviral functions such as IFN-γ and TNF (20)."
>
> — Lee et al. (2019)

> "their function is regulated by the heterogeneous expression of a wide array of surface receptors that shape its phenotypic diversity."
>
> — Mahapatra et al. (2017)

> "NK cells also play a role in regulating other immune and nonimmune cells through the production of a variety of cytokines [3,4]."
>
> — Lee et al. (2019)

### Immunosurveillance & Disease Context

NK cell infiltration and phenotype change in skin disease contexts, such as atopic dermatitis (AD) and cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL):

> "The results showed increased frequencies of both CLA+CD56bright and CLA+CD56dim NK cell populations in the peripheral blood, mainly in severe AD patients."
>
> — de Lima et al. (2024)

> "A reduced fraction of CTCL skin NK cells expressed the maturation marker CD57, the cytotoxic protein granzyme B and the activation marker CD69, indicating reduced tumor-killing abilities of the NK cells."
>
> — Scheffschick et al. (2023)

> "increased numbers of NK cells in CTCL skin exhibit a partially impaired phenotype in terms of activity."
>
> — Scheffschick et al. (2023)

### Developmental and Homeostatic Roles

NK cells contribute to skin homeostasis and may interact with commensal microbiota or participate in early regulation of tissue development:

> "In mice, NK 1.1+ ILC1 cells are highly enriched in fetal and neonatal skin, but rapidly decline within 6 weeks of life (86). These cells are believed to play a significant role in regulating early cutaneous microbiota colonization."
>
> — Johnson et al. (2025)

## Structure / Morphology

> "Natural killer (NK) cells are large granular lymphocytes of the innate immune system that participate in the early control of microbial infections and cancer."
>
> — Grégoire et al. (2007)

> "NK cells were shown to differ from adaptive lymphocytes in respect to their morphology as well as in their capability to kill tumor cells without prior sensitization"
>
> — Carotta et al. (2016)

NK cells are morphologically characterized as large lymphocytes containing cytoplasmic granules, the store of perforin, granzymes, and other effector molecules that underpin their cytotoxic function.
