# Plasma Cells  
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Plasma cells are terminally differentiated B lineage cells specialized for the robust production and secretion of immunoglobulins as part of the humoral immune response. In adult human skin, they represent a minor population, identifiable by high expression of canonical markers such as SDC1, MZB1, CD38, and TNFRSF17 (Table S2). Their primary functional role is to secrete antibodies, typically either IgG or IgA, contributing to local and systemic immunity. Although most abundant in the lamina propria and bone marrow, plasma cells can infiltrate skin during inflammatory responses, immune-mediated diseases, or neoplastic processes, where they may be found in perivascular or interstitial dermal compartments.

## Markers

Plasma cells in human skin and other tissues are characterized by high expression of the following marker genes (Table S2):

- **SDC1 (syndecan-1/CD138):** A transmembrane heparan sulfate proteoglycan, SDC1 is a well-established plasma cell marker, functionally involved in cell adhesion and signaling. Its strong surface expression facilitates immunohistochemical identification and underscores terminal B cell differentiation (Table S2).

- **MZB1:** An endoplasmic reticulum protein involved in proper folding of immunoglobulin heavy chains and secretion of antibodies, emphasizing the plasma cell’s role as an antibody factory (Table S2).

- **CD38:** A multifunctional glycoprotein implicated in calcium signaling, cell adhesion, and regulation of cell activation, further marking antibody-secreting cell identity (Table S2).

- **TNFRSF17 (BCMA):** A receptor important for plasma cell survival, mediating signals from factors such as APRIL and BAFF in the microenvironment to promote longevity (Table S2).

Expression of these markers at high levels distinguishes plasma cells from other B lineage populations (Table S2).

## Location

### Skin (Adult Human)

- Plasma cells are described as a “minor population in adult tissues,” but their presence is detectable in healthy and diseased skin, especially in the dermal compartment (Table S1) and various case studies.
- They are reported in perivascular and interstitial infiltrates of the superficial and deep dermis, sometimes forming dense clusters in inflammatory or neoplastic lesions:
  > "Histology showed a rather dense, non-epidermotropic, interstitial, and perivascular dermal infiltrate involving the superficial and profound dermis (Figure 1). The infiltrate was mainly composed of mature-looking plasma cells with a minor component of small lymphocytes of B-cell origin (Figure 2)."  
  > — Zanelli et al. (2022)

  > "Primary cutaneous plasmacytosis (PCP) is an uncommon cutaneous disorder characterized by the infiltration of plasma cells in the skin, representing a reactive process."  
  > — Nuchanatanon et al. (2024)

- Plasma cell predominance has also been described in conditions such as lichen planus pigmentosus and recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa–associated lesions, indicating an immune response in diverse disease states:
  > "we identified 10 immune cell populations using marker expression patterns (Supplementary Fig. 2b), and found that RDEB skin lesions, in contrast to healthy tissue section, were marked by the presence of neutrophils, B cells, and plasma cells, as well as elevated numbers of activated CD4 + and CD8 + T, regulatory T cells, monocytes/ DC, and macrophages (Fig. 1d)."  
  > — Hirt et al. (2025)

  > "Plasma Cell Predominance in Lichen Planus Pigmentosus: A Rare Case in a Young Female"  
  > — Zhou et al. (2025)

### Other Organs and Systems

- Plasma cells are most abundantly found in the lamina propria of mucosal organs and the bone marrow (Table S1; Bemark et al., 2021):
  > "plasma cells are also found at non-mucosal sites, and IgA antibodies are present in the circulation."  
  > — Bemark et al. (2021)

  > "A relatively large proportion of plasma cells in the bone marrow are IgA producing, both in mice and humans."  
  > — Bemark et al. (2021)

- They also appear in secondary lymphoid tissues and sites of inflammation throughout the body.

## Function

### Antibody Production

- The primary function of plasma cells is abundant antibody secretion:
  > "Antibodies are secreted by plasma cells at a rate of 10 3 antibody molecules/cell per s (1,2)."  
  > — Merville et al. (1996)

  > "Plasma cells (PCs), the B lineage cells responsible for producing and secreting antibodies (Abs), are critical cellular components of the humoral immune system."  
  > — Lindquist et al. (2019)

  > "Their primary role is to synthesize antibodies as a part of the humoral immune response."  
  > — Ciepiela et al. (2021)

  > "In the course of humoral response, polyclonal plasma cells produce polyclonal immunoglobulins with different heavy and light chains."  
  > — Ciepiela et al. (2021)

- Distinct plasma cell subtypes are associated with IgG or IgA production, with spatial specialization in tissues:
  > "this particular cell population predominantly exhibited IgG expression, with a subset displaying IgA (Fig. S7e, f)."  
  > — Jing Ye et al. (2024)

  > "f B-cell receptor gene expression heatmap for the IgA plasma cell spots in the mucosa and the IgG plasma cell voxels in the lesion, normalized across spots."  
  > — Su et al. (2023)

### Immunological Memory and Longevity

- While many plasma cells are short-lived, a subset is long-lived and persists over the lifespan to maintain humoral memory:
  > "While most of the antibody-secreting cells in the body have a rather short lifetime of a few days, some of them can become long-lived and persist in the body over the entire life span of an individual."  
  > — Lindquist et al. (2019)

  > "The majority of these long-lived plasma cells secretes protective antibodies against pathogens, and are thereby crucial for the humoral component of immunological memory."  
  > — Lindquist et al. (2019)

- These cells can contribute to ongoing protection, but when autoreactive, may participate in chronic autoimmune disease:
  > "Although the majority of plasma cells are protective, sometimes long-lived plasma cells produce autoreactive antibodies, which contribute to the pathogenesis and perpetuation of chronic autoimmune diseases, including lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, or multiple sclerosis."  
  > — Lindquist et al. (2019)

### Role in Disease and Immunity

- Increased plasma cells are observed in inflammatory skin conditions, cutaneous infiltrates, and in association with improved outcomes in cancer immunology:
  > "plasma cells adjacent to tumor cells demonstrates its beneficial spatial location to play its further antitumor immunity."  
  > — Jing Ye et al. (2024)
  
  > "within the GSE120575 cohort, we found that a higher plasma cell ratio was associated with improved survival and enhanced immune response (Fig. 2e, f)."  
  > — Jing Ye et al. (2024)

- In the context of primary cutaneous plasmacytosis:
  > "Primary cutaneous plasmacytosis (PCP) is an uncommon cutaneous disorder characterized by the infiltration of plasma cells in the skin, representing a reactive process."  
  > — Nuchanatanon et al. (2024)

- Plasma cells are also components of inflammatory infiltrates in response to infection and tissue injury:
  > "The counts of dermal inflammatory cell infiltrations (neutrophils, plasma cells, mast cells, eosinophils and lymphocytes) were performed in five randomly selected fields at 400× magnification in one section taken from the skin."  
  > — Espinosa et al. (2017)

## Structure / Morphology

- Plasma cells exhibit distinct cellular architecture, facilitating their identification in tissue sections:
  > "The plasma cells typically exhibit eccentric nuclei with abundant basophilic cytoplasm, resembling the appearance of neoplastic plasma cells."  
  > — Nuchanatanon et al. (2024)

  > "Immunohistochemical staining shows positive staining for CD138 and kappa or lambda light chains, confirming the plasma cell origin."  
  > — Nuchanatanon et al. (2024)

  > "The presence of Russell bodies, which are eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions, is a characteristic feature but not always present."  
  > — Nuchanatanon et al. (2024)

- In disease states and neoplastic transformation, plasma cells are non-dividing, mature, and express immunoglobulin light chains:
  > "The plasma cells expressed plasma cell-related markers, such as CD138, and were monotypic for kappa immunoglobulin light chain (Figures 3 and 4)."  
  > — Zanelli et al. (2022)

  > "Plasma cells are non-dividing and fully differentiated cells arising from the B cell line."  
  > — Ciepiela et al. (2021)
