# Migratory Langerhans Cell (LC_4) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [Langerhans cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000453) (CL:0000453, broad match — no exact CL term for migratory LC substate)

## Summary

LC_4 is the migratory Langerhans cell (LC) subset in adult human skin. Reynolds et al. (2021) identify LC4 at the convergence of the LC, myeloid DC, and monocyte-derived DC differentiation branches in PAGA analysis, where it shares a common gene signature with the migratory DC (Mig. DC) cluster and moDC3. This shared signature includes DC maturation markers (CD83, CCR7, LAMP3, CD40, CD86), immunoregulatory molecules (CD274/PD-L1, IDO1, CD200, PDCD1LG2, SOCS1), and migration-associated transcripts (FSCN1, PLGRKT, TRAF1, BCL2A1, CFLAR, REL). Vine et al. (2022), reviewing Liu et al. (2021), define LC4 as the migratory LC state characterised by high CCR7 and CXCR4 expression. Upon acquiring this migratory programme, LC4 loses subset-specific identity genes, reflecting the conserved cross-lineage maturation process described by Reynolds et al. (2021).

## Markers

> "The clusters at the convergence of these branches (moDC3, LC4 and migratory DC (Mig. DC)) express transcripts associated with DC maturation (CD83, CCR7, LAMP3, CD40, CD86), immunoregulation (CD274, IDO1, CD200, PDCD1LG2, SOCS1) and migration (FSCN1, PLGRKT, TRAF1, BCL2A1, CFLAR and REL)"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

> "Recent single cell transcriptomic analysis of the human stratified squamous epithelium has suggested that LCs exist as multiple subsets; two at steady state termed LC1 and LC2, an activated subset defined by high CD83 and low CCR7 expression termed LC3, and a migratory subset defined by high CCR7 expression termed LC4"
>
> — Vine et al. (2022), reviewing Liu et al. (2021)

Key markers of LC4:
- **CCR7** — high expression; drives migration to lymph nodes via CCL19/CCL21
- **CD83** — maturation marker; high expression
- **LAMP3** — maturation/lysosomal marker
- **CD40, CD86** — co-stimulatory molecules
- **FSCN1** — actin-bundling protein; required for DC migration
- **PLGRKT** — plasminogen receptor; promotes cell migration
- **CD274 (PD-L1)** — immunoregulatory ligand
- **IDO1** — immunosuppressive enzyme
- **CXCR4** — chemokine receptor; migratory (Liu framework via Vine et al., 2022)

## Location

LC4 cells are LCs that have exited the epidermis and are actively migrating — via the dermis and afferent lymphatics — to the skin-draining lymph nodes. Reynolds et al. (2021) show that LC4 converges topologically with the migratory DC cluster, which is defined by CCR7-dependent migration to lymph nodes. Upon acquisition of the migratory programme:

> "Acquisition of this common gene signature is associated with loss of genes conferring subset identity in mice, which we also observe here for moDC3, LC4 and Mig. DC"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

## Function

### 1. Skin-to-lymph node migration

LC4 is the migratory LC state defined by high CCR7 expression. CCR7 drives LC migration to skin-draining lymph nodes via the CCL21 gradient expressed by afferent lymphatic endothelial cells. This is a continuous homeostatic process: even in healthy skin, approximately 3% of LCs migrate to lymph nodes per day.

### 2. T cell priming and immune regulation in lymph nodes

Upon reaching the lymph node, LC4 cells are equipped with co-stimulatory molecules (CD40, CD86) for T cell priming and immunoregulatory molecules (PD-L1, IDO1, CD200, SOCS1) for modulating the immune response. Reynolds et al. (2021) note that:

> "Partition-based approximate graph abstraction (PAGA) analysis revealed three branches of differentiation: LCs, myeloid DCs (DC1 and DC2) and monocyte-derived DCs (mo-DCs)"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

The convergence of all three DC lineages at the LC4/Mig. DC/moDC3 node indicates that migratory function is a shared endpoint of DC maturation across different epidermal and dermal DC lineages.

### 3. Conserved migratory programme across disease contexts

Reynolds et al. (2021) demonstrate that the migratory DC signature is not skin-specific but is conserved across multiple tissues and disease states:

> "Surprisingly, the migratory DC signature is also present across disease states including tonsillitis, ascites, lung cancer and rheumatoid arthritis"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

Reynolds et al. (2021) also note previous evidence of migratory gene expression in fetal thymic medullary DCs, suggesting these programmes are utilised during fetal development and re-engaged in adult tissue homeostasis and disease.

## Disease relevance

LC4's migratory programme is augmented in AD and psoriasis. The shared LC4/Mig. DC/moDC3 convergence node in the PAGA graph likely represents an amplified migratory output in inflammatory skin — consistent with the increased LC migration to lymph nodes observed in these diseases, which drives systemic T cell sensitisation.
