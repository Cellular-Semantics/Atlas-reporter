# Undifferentiated Keratinocyte (c1) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)

## Summary

The c1 cluster represents undifferentiated basal keratinocytes in adult human skin, as defined by scRNA-seq profiling in the Reynolds et al. (2021) adult skin atlas. This cell state is characterised by high expression of basal epidermal keratins KRT5 and KRT14, hemidesmosome components (ITGA6, COL17A1), and the stemness transcription factor TP63 (Liu et al., 2022). Undifferentiated keratinocytes reside in the basal layer of the interfollicular epidermis, where they serve as the progenitor pool that sustains lifelong epidermal renewal through self-renewal and differentiation. Trajectory analysis positions these cells at the origin of dual differentiation pathways toward terminally differentiated keratinocytes (Reynolds et al., 2021). The adult skin annotations including c1 were integrated into the Gopee et al. (2024) prenatal skin atlas for cross-developmental comparisons.

## Markers

### Core basal keratins: KRT5 and KRT14

The KRT5–KRT14 keratin pair is the defining molecular signature of undifferentiated basal keratinocytes. Reynolds et al. (2021) identified these as the primary markers distinguishing this population from differentiated keratinocytes:

> "Undifferentiated keratinocytes transcribe basal epidermal proteins (KRT5, KRT14) and are abundant in the CD49fhi FACS gate"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

This finding is robustly replicated across multiple independent scRNA-seq studies. As Zhang et al. (2019) note, the functional significance of this keratin pair extends beyond structural roles:

> "In normal skin epidermis, basal keratinocytes express type I and type II keratin pair KRT14 (K14)-KRT5 (K5), which is necessary for maintaining keratinocyte proliferation by increasing AKT phosphorylation, promoting cell cycle progression and inhibiting spontaneous differentiation of keratinocytes"
>
> — Zhang et al. (2019)

### Stemness factor: TP63

TP63 is a key transcription factor expressed by undifferentiated keratinocytes that acts as a gatekeeper of proliferation in epithelial stem cells. Reynolds et al. (2021) identified it among the stem cell genes enriched in basal keratinocytes, alongside PPP3CA and CAV1/2. The role of TP63 (p63) in controlling the balance between self-renewal and differentiation is further supported by Gerasymchuk et al. (2020):

> "The renewal of the cutaneous barrier occurs via the spinous transition of basal cells in mature skin in parallel with changes in gene expression (e.g., the down-regulation of Keratin5/14 and up-regulation of Keratin1/10) controlled by p63 and the canonical Notch pathway"
>
> — Gerasymchuk et al. (2020)

### Hemidesmosome and adhesion molecules: ITGA6 and COL17A1

Liu et al. (2022) confirmed that undifferentiated basal keratinocytes also express hemidesmosome components that anchor them to the basement membrane:

> "undifferentiated basal keratinocytes had high expression of basal epidermal proteins (KRT5 and KRT14), hemidesmosome molecules [integrin alpha-6, ITGA6; and collagen alpha-1 (XVII) chain, COL17A1] and stemness factor (tumor protein 63, TP63)"
>
> — Liu et al. (2022)

### Additional stem cell genes: PPP3CA, CAV1, CAV2

Reynolds et al. (2021) identified PPP3CA (calcineurin catalytic subunit) and caveolins CAV1 and CAV2 as enriched in the basal keratinocyte compartment, marking the root of differentiation trajectories.

## Location

### Basal layer of the interfollicular epidermis

Undifferentiated keratinocytes reside in the stratum basale, the deepest layer of the epidermis, where they are anchored to the basement membrane via hemidesmosomal integrin complexes. Szabó et al. (2025) describe this compartment:

> "Undifferentiated keratinocytes in the interfollicular epidermis originate from mitotically active cells in the basal layer of the epidermis, where two distinct cell subpopulations, namely keratinocyte stem cells and transit amplifying cells, can be identified"
>
> — Szabó et al. (2025)

In the Reynolds et al. (2021) atlas, undifferentiated keratinocytes are abundant in the CD49f-high (integrin α6-high) FACS gate, consistent with their basal location and strong adhesion to the basement membrane.

## Function

### Self-renewal and epidermal homeostasis

The primary function of undifferentiated keratinocytes is to maintain the regenerative capacity of the epidermis. The basal layer contains two functionally distinct subpopulations. Szabó et al. (2025) describe:

> "Keratinocyte stem cells are capable of nearly unlimited self-renewal, though they typically exist in a quiescent state and rarely divide"
>
> — Szabó et al. (2025)

> "Transit-amplifying cells, which originate from stem cells, demonstrate high proliferative capacity and multipotency, undergoing multiple rounds of cell division prior to differentiation"
>
> — Szabó et al. (2025)

Together, these populations ensure a constant supply of keratinocytes to replace those lost through terminal differentiation and desquamation at the skin surface.

### Origin of dual differentiation trajectories

A key finding from Reynolds et al. (2021) is that undifferentiated basal keratinocytes give rise to two distinct differentiation pathways:

> "Force-directed graph (FDG) and PAGA analyses reveal dual inferred differentiation trajectories from the stem cell genes (TP63, PPP3CA and CAV1/2)-enriched basal keratinocytes into terminally differentiated keratinocytes expressing CNFN, FLG and IVL"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

These two trajectories are distinguished by their expression of lamellar body-related transcripts:

> "One arm expresses high levels of lamellar body (LB)-related transcripts such as ABCA12, CKAP4 and CLIP1 that characterize late epidermal differentiation and the other arm lower levels of LB-related transcripts"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

The transcription factors IRF1 and SOX9 are differentially expressed between these two pathways and were validated by immunofluorescence in healthy skin (Reynolds et al., 2021). This dual trajectory model represents a more nuanced view of epidermal differentiation than the classical single-pathway model.

Polito et al. (2023) corroborate this hierarchical organisation:

> "Trajectory analysis suggested that the undifferentiated cluster contains SCs, which give rise to proliferating progenitors and differentiated cells"
>
> — Polito et al. (2023)

### Relevance to disease

In the Reynolds et al. (2021) atlas, lesional psoriasis and atopic dermatitis skin showed altered undifferentiated keratinocyte gene expression, with lower expression of stem cell and basal keratinocyte genes (CAV1/2, KRT14, DUSP10) and higher expression of commitment genes (FOS, JUNB, CDKN1A, MAFB), suggesting rapid transition and premature differentiation of keratinocytes in inflammatory skin disease.
