# Regulatory T Cell in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [regulatory T cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000815) (CL:0000815, exact match)

## Summary

Regulatory T cells (Treg) in adult human skin are one of three T cell subtypes identified by Reynolds et al. (2021). They are defined by expression of FOXP3, TIGIT, and CTLA4, and represent the immunosuppressive arm of the adaptive immune response. Dermal Tregs in adult skin show high expression of CD62L (SELL), a circulating central memory T cell marker, distinguishing them phenotypically from epidermal T cells. Tregs are essential for maintaining skin immune homeostasis and peripheral tolerance to commensal antigens. Note: this report describes the adult Treg population; the fetal Treg report is documented separately (Treg_fetal.md).

## Markers

> "regulatory (Treg) expressing FOXP3, TIGIT, CTLA4"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

> "Tregs expressed FOXP3, CTLA4 and IL2RA, defining their immune-modulatory function"
>
> — Marella et al. (2024)

A key feature distinguishing dermal Tregs from other T cell subsets in adult skin is their CD62L expression:

> "Dermal Tregs show high mRNA and protein expression of the circulating central memory T cells marker CD62L (SELL)"
>
> — Reynolds et al. (2021)

## Location

Tregs are found primarily in the dermis of adult human skin. They comprise approximately 20% of skin-resident CD4+ T cells:

> "In adult healthy human skin, approximately 20% of tissue-resident CD4+ T-cells are T regs T rm expressing the transcription factor Foxp3, and only 5% of T regs recirculate"
>
> — Cañedo-Dorantes and Cañedo-Ayala (2019)

Skin-homing Tregs express CCR4 and CLA (cutaneous lymphocyte antigen), which enable their migration from blood to skin:

> "In human peripheral blood, most Treg cells express functional skin-homing receptors such as the functional E-selectin ligand cutaneous lymphocyte antigen (CLA) and CCR4"
>
> — Gratz and Campbell (2014)

## Function

### Immune tolerance and homeostasis

Tregs suppress excessive immune responses against commensal microorganisms and self-antigens in skin:

> "Skin homeostasis and peripheral tolerance to commensals and self-antigens are controlled by skin immunosuppressive CD4+Foxp3+ regulatory T cells (T regs) that suppress the abnormal effects of self-reactive immune cells' responses"
>
> — Cañedo-Dorantes and Cañedo-Ayala (2019)

> "multiple studies have demonstrated that Treg cell migration to the skin is essential for their ability to prevent inflammatory disease in the skin"
>
> — Gratz and Campbell (2014)

### Wound healing and tissue repair

Tregs contribute to wound re-epithelialisation by upregulating epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) expression, and they modulate tissue inflammation by limiting IFN production and reducing inflammatory macrophage numbers (Cañedo-Dorantes and Cañedo-Ayala, 2019).

### Role in inflammatory skin disease

In AD and psoriasis, Treg populations are expanded in lesional skin relative to non-lesional skin (Reynolds et al., 2021). Bulk RNA-seq deconvolution data confirmed Treg enrichment in lesional AD dermis (Reynolds et al., 2021). However, the interaction between inflammatory monocytes and Tregs can be dysregulated in pathological contexts, leading to loss of Treg suppressive function (West et al., 2022).
