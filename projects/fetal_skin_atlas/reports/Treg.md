# Regulatory T Cell (Treg)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Regulatory T cells (Tregs), also known as Regulatory T cells or Tregs, constitute a crucial immunosuppressive CD4+ T cell subset in human skin with distinct marker expression and functional versatility (Name Resolution). Characterized primarily by the expression of Foxp3, high CD25, and other molecules such as CTLA-4, CD39, and ICOS, Tregs are found in significant numbers within the adult skin, particularly enriched in the dermis and around hair follicles. Their main roles include maintenance of peripheral immune tolerance, suppression of inflammation, tissue repair, and support of non-immunological processes in the skin. These cells display substantial phenotypic heterogeneity and adapt their phenotype based on tissue localization and local environmental cues.

## Markers

Tregs are defined by the expression of several key markers, each contributing to or reflecting specific functional properties:

- **Foxp3 (Forkhead box P3)**: This transcription factor is the master regulator of Treg development and suppressive function. Its high expression defines thymus-derived Tregs (tTregs), while a stably differentiated phenotype is maintained through demethylation at the Foxp3 promoter (H. Lei et al., 2015; J. Khantakova et al., 2022).

- **CD25 (IL-2 receptor alpha chain)**: High surface expression of CD25 is characteristic of Tregs and used in their identification. Functionally, it enhances sensitivity to IL-2, supporting Treg survival and activation (A. Chiricozzi et al., 2018).

- **CD39**: An ectoenzyme involved in ATP/AMP hydrolysis and anti-inflammatory adenosine production, frequently co-expressed with CD25 on skin-resident Tregs (A. Salminen et al., 2022; R. Ikebuchi et al., 2016).

- **CTLA-4 (Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte Antigen 4)**: An inhibitory receptor implicated in Treg-mediated suppression of effector T cell responses. Its higher surface expression is a hallmark of activated/memory Tregs (Xiaohui Zhou et al., 2015; Amit Sharma et al., 2018).

- **CD45RO, CD27, BCL-2**: These are memory markers found on adult skin-resident Tregs, indicating prior antigen experience and long-term persistence (Samuel Knoedler et al., 2023; J. Khantakova et al., 2022).

- **ICOS (Inducible T-cell COStimulator)**: Expressed on activated skin Tregs and linked to proliferation and heightened suppressive function (Xiaohui Zhou et al., 2015; Amit Sharma et al., 2018).

- **CLA (Cutaneous Lymphocyte Antigen)**: Mediates skin homing; Tregs expressing CLA are more effective at migrating to and functionally suppressing responses in the skin (F. Issa et al., 2012).

- **CCR4, CCR6, CCR8, and CXCR3**: Chemokine receptors that guide Treg migration and tissue localization (F. Issa et al., 2012; N. Beumer et al., 2025).

- **CD127 (IL-7R)**: Low or absent on most Tregs, helping to distinguish them from other T cell populations (T. Reduta et al., 2023).

- **EGFR**: Expressed by Tregs involved in tissue repair (Amit Sharma et al., 2018).

- **Granzyme B, IL-10, TGF-β, Perforin**: Effector molecules that mediate immune suppression and cytolytic activity (A. Chiricozzi et al., 2018; R. Ikebuchi et al., 2016).

> "The immunosuppressive genes Ctla4 and Tgfb1 were expressed in the majority of Tregs."  
> — R. Ikebuchi et al. (2016)

> "mTreg cells in human skin express memory markers (CD27 and BCL-2) and elevated amounts of activation markers (CTLA4, CD25, and ICOS)."  
> — J. Khantakova et al. (2022)

> "Foxp3 + Treg can furthermore be induced from naïve conventional T cells (Tconv) in the presence of specific cytokines and low amounts of antigens in the periphery (Furtado et al., 2002;Apostolou and von Boehmer, 2004;Curotto de Lafaille et al., 2004), to yield the so-called induced Treg (iTreg)."  
> — H. Lei et al. (2015)

> "Previous studies have shown the presence of the cutaneous lymphocyte antigen (CLA) on 68-90% of CD4CD25highFoxp3 lymphocytes in peripheral blood of healthy adults. This antigen is responsible for Treg migration to the skin [67]."  
> — T. Reduta et al. (2023)

## Location

### Skin Compartments and Microanatomy

Tregs are highly represented in adult skin, distributed in both dermal and epidermal compartments, with enriched localization around hair follicles—the latter serving as a key niche:

> "healthy skin in humans and mice contains numerous Tregs, predominantly restricted to the dermis"  
> — M. Norman et al. (2021)

> "Skin-resident Tregs preferentially localize to the epidermis and dermis around HF, a wellknown reservoir of commensal microbiota and stem cells"  
> — Samuel Knoedler et al. (2023)

> "Collectively, immune-suppressive CD4+CD103−Foxp3+ Tregs are present in the normal, noninflamed human epidermis and mucosal epithelia."  
> — Takuya Sato et al. (2022)

### Trafficking and Circulation

Tregs display both resident and migratory behavior, shuttling between skin, blood, and draining lymph nodes:

> "Tregs in both skin and dLNs comprised two subpopulations, one highly expressing Nrp1 with variable CD25, Granzyme B, and/or CTLA-4 expression and another with 3 subsets strongly expressing CD25, Granzyme B, or CTLA-4 together with CD39."  
> — R. Ikebuchi et al. (2019)

> "Treg circulate between blood, skin, and lymphoid tissues and...local Treg proliferation takes place in order to regulate peripheral skin immune responses."  
> — V. L. de Oliveira et al. (2012)

> "our data indicate that blood CCR8+ Treg cells contain recirculating human skin Treg cells."  
> — N. Beumer et al. (2025)

### Abundance

Tregs account for a substantial proportion of CD4+ T cells in the skin—ranging from 5–20% in adult human skin, and even higher in mice (20–60%) (A. Salminen et al., 2022; V. L. de Oliveira et al., 2012).

### Developmental and Dynamic Aspects

- Nearly all skin Tregs in adults are of a memory phenotype (CD45RO+), compared with the naïve phenotype prevalent in fetal skin (J. Khantakova et al., 2022).
- Their number increases with age and during inflammatory responses (A. Salminen et al., 2022).

## Function

### Immune Suppression and Homeostasis

Tregs are master regulators of immune homeostasis, preventing autoimmunity and excessive inflammation, and mediating tolerance to self and commensal antigens:

> "Regulatory T cells (Tregs) are essential for maintaining immune homeostasis."  
> — R. Ikebuchi et al. (2019)

> "Skin Treg have been shown to facilitate cutaneous wound healing and epithelial stem cell differentiation through expression of Jagged 1 (116), and to regulate fibroblast activation and fibrosis through expression of a skewed transcriptional programme dominated by GATA3 (117)."  
> — M. Sambucci et al. (2019)

> "Regulatory T cells (Tregs) are a T cell subset that exerts a potent immunosuppressive effect to inhibit auto-reactive T cell activation."  
> — T. Honda et al. (2016)

> "Tregs suppress inflammation by inhibiting the effector functions of CD4 + T lymphocytes, namely, Th1, Th2 and Th17 cells, as well as CD8 + T lymphocytes [78]."  
> — K. Raziyeva et al. (2021)

Tregs suppress immune activity by:

- Producing anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-10, TGF-β, IL-35)
- Expressing inhibitory receptors (CTLA-4)
- Secreting cytolytic molecules (perforin, granzyme B)
- Generating immunosuppressive metabolites (via CD39/CD73-mediated adenosine production)  
- Modulating dendritic cell and macrophage function

> "Tregs suppress an immune response via a number of different mechanisms. Those mechanisms include an expression of anti-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-10, TGF-β and IL-35, as well as secretion of apoptotic factors, granzyme and perforin. Other mechanisms involve the production of CD39/CD73 and regulation of CTLA-4 [88]."  
> — K. Raziyeva et al. (2021)

### Tissue Repair and Non-immune Roles

Skin Tregs participate in wound healing, tissue regeneration, and maintaining the integrity of the skin barrier:

> "Skin Treg have been shown to facilitate cutaneous wound healing and epithelial stem cell differentiation through expression of Jagged 1 (116), and to regulate fibroblast activation and fibrosis through expression of a skewed transcriptional programme dominated by GATA3 (117)."  
> — M. Sambucci et al. (2019)

> "Treg depletion compromised normal acute wound healing. Specifically, it led to impaired re-epithelization, reduced wound closure and delayed vessel maturation [87]."  
> — K. Raziyeva et al. (2021)

### Antigen Specificity and Memory

Skin Tregs exhibit a strong memory phenotype, persist long after antigen clearance, and suppress inflammation upon re-challenge:

> "The antigen-specific memory Treg lymphocytes have been also found in the skin. They persisted in the skin long after antigen removal and resolution of inflammation. Upon antigen re-exposure, these cells were responsible for suppressing the inflammation."  
> — T. Reduta et al. (2023)

> "Such memory Tregs have also been detected in human skin, with comparatively low proliferative index and non-migratory, anergic properties under steady-state conditions [110]."  
> — Samuel Knoedler et al. (2023)

### Role in Disease and Therapy

Aberrant Treg function or abundance can contribute to autoimmune skin diseases (e.g., psoriasis, vitiligo, atopic dermatitis) and cancer, while therapeutic modulation of Tregs has shown efficacy in transplantation, autoimmune disease, and wound healing models:

> "abnormal function of Tregs is relevant to the pathogenesis of several skin diseases like psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, systemic lupus erythematosus, cutaneous T-cell lymphomas, and skin cancer and is also important in rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes and other autoimmune diseases."  
> — B. Nedoszytko et al. (2017)

> "human Treg injection inhibits skin inflammation and the influx of effector T cells."  
> — Sija Landman et al. (2020)

## Structure / Morphology

Skin Tregs generally exhibit an effector-memory phenotype (CD45RO+, CD27+, BCL-2+), are slow-cycling, and show low proliferative indices under steady-state conditions. In the skin, they are often clustered around hair follicles and display a heterogeneous morphology reflecting their activation and tissue adaptation status:

> "Such memory Tregs have also been detected in human skin, with comparatively low proliferative index and non-migratory, anergic properties under steady-state conditions [110]."  
> — Samuel Knoedler et al. (2023)

> "The resident Treg cells of human skin are a dynamic and heterogeneous population of the FoxP3-containing T cells."  
> — A. Salminen et al. (2022)
