# ILC1_NK (ILC1/NK cell, innate lymphoid cell type 1/natural killer cell)

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))  
Scope: adult

## Summary

ILC1_NK cells, also annotated as "ILC1/NK cell" or "innate lymphoid cell type 1/natural killer cell", comprise a subset of innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) identified in adult human skin. These cells bridge the properties of conventional natural killer (NK) cells and type 1 helper-like innate lymphoid cells (ILC1s), sharing key markers such as T-bet, NKp46, and, variably, Eomes, while diverging in cytotoxic capabilities and marker expression. They are tissue-resident lymphocytes that are especially enriched at barrier surfaces such as skin, where they contribute to immune defense primarily via cytokine production and, for NK cells, cytotoxicity. The phenotypic distinction between ILC1 and NK cells is nuanced in human tissues, as they share several transcriptional and surface markers.

## Markers

The molecular identification of ILC1_NK cells is defined by a combination of transcription factors, cytokine profiles, and surface markers. The following evidence details these markers and their significance:

- **T-bet (TBX21):** Both ILC1s and NK cells require the transcription factor T-bet for development and function.
  
  > "NK cells and ILC1 are cells that produce interferon-g (IFN-g) as their signature cytokine and need the T-bet transcription factor to develop."
  >
  > — Orrantia et al. (2020)

- **Eomesodermin (EOMES):** Eomes marks NK cells but is absent in canonical ILC1s.
  
  > "the expressions of EOMES, GZMA, IRF8, JAK1, NKG7, PLEK, PRF1, and ZEB2 define NK cells and that IL7R, LTB, and RGS1 differentiate ILC1s from NK cells in mice and humans."
  >
  > — Lopes et al. (2022)
  
  > "ILC1s share several similarities with cNK cells but are not as cytotoxic and do not express Eomes [1]."
  >
  > — Molgora et al. (2021)

- **CD127 (IL7Rα):** Highly expressed on ILC1s, but generally low or negative on mature NK cells, distinguishing tissue-resident ILC1s from NKs.

  > "ILC1 does not express CD117 (c-kit) or NKp44, but highly expresses CD127 (IL-7Rα), which distinguishes it from immature NK cells."
  >
  > — Bento et al. (2024)
  
- **Perforin (PRF1) and Granzymes (GZMA/B):** Markers of cytotoxic function, enriched in NK cells and largely absent or low in canonical ILC1s.
  
  > "the expressions of EOMES, GZMA, IRF8, JAK1, NKG7, PLEK, PRF1, and ZEB2 define NK cells and that IL7R, LTB, and RGS1 differentiate ILC1s from NK cells in mice and humans."
  >
  > — Lopes et al. (2022)
  
  > "ILC1 exhibit very little or no cytotoxic activity due to the low or zero levels of perforin and granzymes they express (Fuchs, 2016;Spits et al., 2016)."
  >
  > — Orrantia et al. (2020)

- **Surface Markers:**
  - **NKp46 and NKG2D:** Shared by both NK cells and ILC1s, complicating their distinction.
    
    > "Although ILC1s expressed a similar array of killer receptors as NK cells, such as NKp46, NKG2D, and NK1.1, the functional implications of these receptors for ILC1 biology remain understudied"
    >
    > — Klose et al. (2020)
  - **CD56:** Expressed on many NK cells, but not always present on ILC1s.
    
    > "Two thirds of the lineage negative cells were CD127 − , a population of cells largely positive for NK and the ILC1 markers CD56 and NKp46."
    >
    > — Dutzan et al. (2015)
  - **CD161 and CD94:** CD161 can mark ILC1s and some NK subsets, while CD94 is typically NK-specific.

    > "Within the original description, ILC1 were defined as lin -CD127 + CD117 -CRTH2 -CD161 + cells (Bernink et al., 2013). However, CD161 is also an NK cell 'marker' and CD127 is consistently expressed on the CD56 bright NK cell subset (Vivier et al., 2018)."
    >
    > — Bennstein et al. (2020)
  - **CD117 (c-kit), NKp44, CRTH2:** Typically not expressed on ILC1s, used to distinguish from other ILC subsets.

    > "ILC1 does not express CD117 (c-kit) or NKp44, but highly expresses CD127 (IL-7Rα), which distinguishes it from immature NK cells."
    >
    > — Bento et al. (2024)

## Location

### Skin and Barrier Tissues

ILC1_NK cells are particularly enriched at barrier surfaces, including the skin.

- According to the atlas, the ILC1_NK annotation in adult skin maps to both "ILC1/NK cell" and "innate lymphoid cell type 1/natural killer cell", consistent across immune cell cluster schematics and fine cell type tables (Supplementary findings from the atlas; Lopes et al., 2022).

- The literature further supports tissue residency for ILC1s at epithelial barriers:
  > "ILC1s are enriched in liver, skin, and gut"
  >
  > — Kuo et al. (2025)

- Evidence from mucosal tissues such as gingiva indicates similar classification by marker expression and cytokine secretion:
  > "Based on functional characteristics oral ILC belong primarily to the ILC1/NK group as they were largely IFNγ + (Fig. 4b). Two thirds of the lineage negative cells were CD127 − , a population of cells largely positive for NK and the ILC1 markers CD56 and NKp46."
  >
  > — Dutzan et al. (2015)

### Other Tissues

ILC1 and NK cells are found in a broad array of tissues, including but not limited to liver, intestine, lung, and lymph nodes, with their specific localization and abundance modulated by tissue cues and local microenvironment (Molgora et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2019; Lopes et al., 2022).

## Function

### Cytokine Production

A defining function of ILC1 and NK cells is their rapid production of the signature cytokine IFN-γ, which orchestrates local immune responses and defense against pathogens.

> "NK cells and ILC1 are cells that produce interferon-g (IFN-g) as their signature cytokine and need the T-bet transcription factor to develop."
>
> — Orrantia et al. (2020)

> "ILC1s resemble Th1 cells as they produce IFNγ and express the transcription factor Tbet."
>
> — Molgora et al. (2021)

### Cytotoxicity

Conventional NK cells exhibit potent cytolytic activity, mediated by the release of perforin and granzymes. In contrast, canonical ILC1s display limited or absent cytotoxic function, instead prioritizing cytokine release.

> "NK cells are cytotoxic and circulate systemically, whereas helper ILCs (ILC1, ILC2, ILC3 and LTi) are predominantly tissue-resident and coordinate local tissue-specific immunity (6)."
>
> — Kuo et al. (2025)

> "ILC1s share several similarities with cNK cells but are not as cytotoxic and do not express Eomes [1]."
>
> — Molgora et al. (2021)

> "While conventional NK cells are considered to possess dual effector functions of both cytokine secretion and cytotoxicity, ILC1s are often viewed as potent cytokine secretors with poor cytolytic capacity [105,110]."
>
> — Liu et al. (2019)

### Immunological Plasticity

There is demonstrated plasticity between ILC1 and NK cell states, influenced by cytokines (e.g., IL-12, IL-15, IL-2), local microenvironmental cues, and other factors. This imparts functional heterogeneity and adaptability, particularly in contexts of tissue inflammation or tumor microenvironment.

> "cytokines, such as IL-12/IL-15, IL-12/IL-2 favor ILC1/NK plasticity"
>
> — Rethacker et al. (2022)

> "Some studies suggest cNK cells can differentiate into ILC1-like cells via IL-12 and TGFb signaling following suppression of Eomes expression, with a simultaneous increase in T-bet expression (46)."
>
> — Johnson and Lee (2025)

### Barrier and Homeostatic Immunity

Both ILC1 and NK cells are integral to immune surveillance at barrier sites (skin, gut, lung) and contribute to tissue homeostasis.

> "ILCs/NK cells mirror the effector programs of adaptive CD4 + T helper (Th) and CD8 + cytotoxic T cells, producing rapid cytokine and effector responses to local environmental cues (1,2)."
>
> — Kuo et al. (2025)

> "NK cells and ILC1 contribute to organ homeostasis through the production of key cytokines and chemokines and the elimination of potential harmful bacteria and viruses."
>
> — Jacquelot et al. (2021)

## Structure / Morphology

NK cells are traditionally described as large granular lymphocytes, though specific morphological distinction of ILC1s from NK cells remains poorly characterized in human tissues. There is general agreement that ILC1s are not as granular or cytotoxic as NK cells, and are more tissue-resident in nature (Molgora et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2019; Vienne et al., 2021).

No additional ultrastructural detail is reported in the cited skin atlas or available literature.
