# Matrix
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Cell Ontology: [hair germinal matrix cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0002562) (CL:0002562, exact match)  
Scope: prenatal (fetal)

## Summary

Hair matrix cells are the highly proliferative transit-amplifying progenitors located at the base of the hair follicle bulb, surrounding the dermal papilla. They arise from the epidermal placode and differentiate into the inner root sheath and hair shaft lineages. In the prenatal skin atlas, matrix cells are identified by SHH expression and are present from 14 PCW (Gopee et al., 2024). Compared with adult hair follicles, prenatal matrix cells show increased expression of immunomodulatory genes such as CXCL14 and CD24, suggesting a role in immune protection during early follicle development.

## Markers

- **SHH (Sonic hedgehog)**: Defining marker of matrix cells in the atlas.
  > "we identified HF cells from 14 PCW in our scRNA-seq data, which comprised placode, matrix (SHH + ), outer root sheath (ORS) (SLC26A7 + ), companion layer (CL), inner root sheath (IRS) and cuticle and cortex cells (cuticle/cortex; part of the inner layers of the HF)"
  > -- Gopee et al. (2024)

- **WNT10B**: Known matrix marker upregulated along the IRS trajectory.
  > "AGR2 was downregulated along the IRS trajectory, whereas known matrix markers such as SHH and WNT10B 25,26 were upregulated"
  > -- Gopee et al. (2024)

- **CXCL14**: Chemokine with increased expression in prenatal matrix cells, reported to recruit regulatory T cells.
  > "prenatal skin matrix cells had increased expression of genes involved in chemotaxis, such as CXCL14, a chemokine previously reported to recruit regulatory T (T reg ) cells"
  > -- Gopee et al. (2024)

- **CD24**: Involved in control of autoimmunity, upregulated in prenatal matrix.

## Developmental Trajectory

Matrix cells arise from the placode along the IRS trajectory:

> "Inferred trajectory and pseudotime analysis of epidermal cells in the integrated prenatal skin and SkO data predicted the differentiation of POSTN + basal cells into two paths: ORS/CL trajectory, comprising DPYSL2 + basal cells, ORS and CL; and IRS trajectory, involving placode, matrix, cuticle/cortex and IRS"
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

Matrix cells represent a prenatal-specific derivative of the placode:

> "We evaluated hair matrix cells, which arise from the epidermal placode, a prenatal-specific cell state absent in established adult HFs"
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

## Signalling

The Dp coordinates matrix cell differentiation via RSPO3-LGR4/LGR6:

> "RSPO3 from the Dp was predicted to interact with LGR4 and LGR6 (Fig. 2g, Extended Data Fig. 5b and Supplementary Table 8) in overlying matrix cells to contribute to the proliferation of HF epithelial cells"
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

## Location

Matrix cells are found surrounding the dermal papilla at the base of the hair follicle bulb, first appearing at 14 PCW in prenatal skin.

## Function

Matrix cells are transit-amplifying progenitors that divide rapidly and differentiate upward to form six concentric cell lineages: three of the inner root sheath (Henle, Huxley, IRS cuticle) and three of the hair shaft (cuticle, cortex, medulla). Their immunomodulatory gene expression may facilitate immune tolerance during early follicle development:

> "This result highlighted the potential role of T reg cell accumulation and immune protection in the early stages of matrix differentiation"
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

## Disease Relevance

> "Genes harbouring mutations known to cause reduced hair growth (hypotrichosis) or abnormally shaped hair (for example, pili torti) (Supplementary Table 12) were expressed along the ORS/CL trajectory, IRS trajectory and hair fibroblast trajectory pseudotimes"
> -- Gopee et al. (2024)

## Structure / Morphology

Matrix cells are located in the hair bulb and are characterized by high mitotic activity. They maintain contact with the dermal papilla and differentiate terminally as they migrate upward.
