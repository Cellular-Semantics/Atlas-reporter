# Schwann Cells (Non-Myelinating)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Non-myelinating Schwann cells are a major glial cell population within the peripheral nervous system, present in greater abundance than their myelinating counterparts, particularly in the skin and peripheral nerves (Li Zhao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). In adult skin, these cells are associated predominantly with dermal and subcutaneous nerve fibers, as well as specialized structures such as nerve terminals and sensory endings (Gresset et al., 2015). They express a range of markers, notably p75 (NGFR), Ncam1, S100, L1CAM, and others that distinguish them from myelinating or immature Schwann cells (Li Zhao et al., 2024; En Yang et al., 2025; Hörner et al., 2022). Functionally, non-myelinating Schwann cells are essential for axon maintenance and neuronal survival, play key roles in regeneration, and serve as integral components of cutaneous sensory structures. They exhibit notable plasticity during injury and can dedifferentiate into repair cells, emphasizing their dynamic contribution to tissue homeostasis and response.

## Markers

Non-myelinating Schwann cells are characterized by the stable expression of several molecular markers:

- **NGFR/p75**: This neurotrophin receptor is a canonical marker for non-myelinating (and immature) Schwann cells. It contributes to cell signaling pathways involved in glial maintenance and response to nerve injury (Li Zhao et al., 2024; Hörner et al., 2022).
- **Ncam1**: The neural cell adhesion molecule 1 is highly expressed in non-myelinating Schwann cells, marking their transition from immature states and supporting axon-glia interactions (Li Zhao et al., 2024).
- **S100**: A glial calcium-binding protein found broadly in Schwann cells, with S100 positivity typical but not exclusive to non-myelinating subtypes (Gresset et al., 2015; Hörner et al., 2022).
- **Gap43 and p75NTR**: Both proteins remain highly expressed in non-myelinating fates, supporting roles in growth and glial signaling (Hörner et al., 2022).
- **NRXN1 (Neurexin 1)**: Expressed as a distinctive marker for non-myelinating Schwann cells, implicated in synaptic organization (Lite Yang et al., 2025).
- **L1CAM and SCN7A**: These molecules specifically characterize non-myelinating Schwann cell subsets and distinguish them from myelinating counterparts (En Yang et al., 2025).
- **Glul, Apod, Smoc2, Fabp7**: Additional marker genes found in non-myelinating Schwann cells, relevant in metabolic and structural regulation (Zhang et al., 2025).

> "Ncam1 and Ngfr/p75 are typical markers of immature Schwann cells, Schwann cells in late embryonic period and early postnatal period. Immature Schwann cells develop to myelinating and non-myelinating Schwann cells postnatally (Jessen and Mirsky, 2019)."
>
> — Li Zhao et al. (2024)

> "expression of Gap43 and p75NTR ceases; however, both persist in the non-myelinating fate [1][2][3]26,65,78,298]"
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

> "nonmyelinating Schwann cells express NRXN1 encoding Neurexin 1, a cell adhesion molecule important for the formation and function of synapses and a marker gene for non-myelinating Schwann cells."
>
> — Lite Yang et al. (2025)

> "SC-Nonmyel was defined by the expression of SCN7A and L1CAM, markers characteristic of non-myelinating Schwann cells, while SC-Myel was identif ied based on MBP, MPZ, PRX, and GLDN, which are typical markers of myelinating Schwann cells [16][17][18]."
>
> — En Yang et al. (2025)

> "as well as nonmyelinating Schwann cells that express p75, glutamate-ammonia ligase (Glul), apolipoprotein D (Apod), SPARC related modular calcium binding 2 (Smoc2), and fatty acid binding protein 7 (Fabp7) (Avraham et al., 2020;Wolbert et al., 2020)."
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

## Location

### Skin and Peripheral Nerves

Non-myelinating Schwann cells predominate in both subcutaneous and dermal nerves of the skin (Gresset et al., 2015). They are closely associated with nerve termini, including lanceolate endings (sensory structures around hair follicles) and free nerve endings responsible for transmitting pain and touch.

> "Schwann cells (mainly non-myelinating) associated with subcutaneous and dermal nerves, and two types of terminal Schwann cells, associated with lanceolate endings or free nerve endings."
>
> — Gresset et al. (2015)

> "They could be classified into three categories as follows: (1) those associated with dermal nerve fibers ( Figures 3A and 3E, closed arrows), (2) lanceolate endings around hair follicles (Figures 3C, 3D, and 3F, empty arrowheads), and (3) free nerve endings of nociceptive fibers ( Figures 3C, 3D, and 3G, closed arrowheads)."
>
> — Gresset et al. (2015)

### Broader Anatomical Distribution

While the atlas focuses on skin, non-myelinating Schwann cells are a fundamental component wherever peripheral axons are present, including the autonomic and sensory ganglia, neuromuscular junctions, sensory corpuscles, and even lymphoid organs such as the spleen (O. Ioghen et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2018).

> "The NMSCs encompass two major cell types, according to their distribution: Schwann cells of Remak fibers and the specialized perisynaptic/terminal Schwann cells at neuromuscular junctions (NMJ). In addition in this category are also included the glial cells found in some sensory transducers, such as the Pacinian and Meissner's corpuscles, as well as in the sensory and autonomic ganglia, where they are called satellite cells [1]."
>
> — O. Ioghen et al. (2020)

> "Distribution of non-myelinating Schwann cells and their associations with leukocytes in mouse spleen revealed by immunofluorescence staining"
>
> — Ma et al. (2018)

### Developmental Timing

Non-myelinating Schwann cells arise postnatally from immature Schwann cells, a process involving dynamic fate choices that can be reversed under injury or disease.

> "Immature Schwann cells develop to myelinating and non-myelinating Schwann cells postnatally (Jessen and Mirsky, 2019)."
>
> — Li Zhao et al. (2024)

## Function

### Axonal Support and Maintenance

Non-myelinating Schwann cells are essential for axon maintenance and overall neuronal survival in the peripheral nervous system.

> "non-myelinating Schwann cells (NMSCs) represent an important category that was not extensively studied, although the gathered data demonstrate they are essential for axon maintenance and neuronal survival in the peripheral nervous system (PNS)."
>
> — O. Ioghen et al. (2020)

> "Remak cells envelop all the small-diameter axons, including axons of the autonomic nervous system. These cells promote the correct development of the PNS and exert regeneration activity after an injury by promoting axonal plasticity, growth, and sprouting [13]."
>
> — Maugeri et al. (2021)

### Tissue Repair and Plasticity

Non-myelinating Schwann cells participate in peripheral nerve regeneration following injury, with the ability to dedifferentiate into repair Schwann cells and later reacquire their mature (myelinating or non-myelinating) fate.

> "Injury of peripheral nerves causes dedifferentiation of both types of mature Schwann cells (myelinating and nonmyelinating) into repair Schwann cells [46][47][48] ."
>
> — Direder et al. (2022)

> "Following the resolution of nerve damage, repair Schwann cells re-differentiate into mature myelinating and non-myelinating Schwann cells. During this re-differentiation process, prior mature myelinating Schwann cells can assume a mature non-myelinating phenotype and vice versa [105,106]."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

### Sensory and Synaptic Modulation

Within the skin, non-myelinating Schwann cells are integral parts of specialized sensory structures, supporting and modulating input from mechanoreceptors and nociceptors.

> "Non-myelinating Schwann cells can be further divided into Remak Schwann cells, terminal Schwann cells at neuromuscular junctions, and Schwann cells in sensory corpuscles (Griffin and Thompson, 2008)."
>
> — Zhang et al. (2025)

> "Lanceolate endings are circumferential structures that surround the hair follicle and are composed of mechanoreceptive nerve fibers and their associated terminal Schwann cells (Li and Ginty, 2014)."
>
> — Gresset et al. (2015)

### Immune Modulation

There is evidence that non-myelinating Schwann cells interact with immune cells, suggesting roles in neuroimmune communication.

> "Distribution of non-myelinating Schwann cells and their associations with leukocytes in mouse spleen revealed by immunofluorescence staining"
>
> — Ma et al. (2018)

## Structure / Morphology

Non-myelinating Schwann cells differ from their myelinating counterparts in that they do not form compact myelin sheaths. Instead, Remak Schwann cells ensheath multiple small-diameter axons, forming Remak bundles, unlike the 1:1 ratio seen in myelinating Schwann cells. Terminal Schwann cells, by contrast, are found at nerve endings and exhibit distinct morphologies tailored to their roles at neuromuscular junctions or sensory structures.

> "non-myelinating Remak Schwann cells...associate with several small diameter axons, and ensheath them in so-called Remak bundles, without producing myelin."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

> "An immature Schwann cell which ensheaths many small axons becomes mature non-myelinating Schwann cell, forming a Remak bundle."
>
> — O. Ioghen et al. (2020)

> "Terminal (or perisynaptic) Schwann cells are a third distinct subtype of Schwann cells. They are associated with neuromuscular junctions, and although they are non-myelinating, terminal Schwann cells differ considerably from Remak Schwann cells in terms of their marker expression, appearance, and function [7,88,89]."
>
> — Hörner et al. (2022)

> "they expressed the Schwann cell marker S100 ( Figure 3F), but not the myelin marker MBP ( Figure 3B), and were, therefore, immature/non-myelinating Schwann cells."
>
> — Gresset et al. (2015)
