# Monocyte (Mono)  
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis  
Scope: adult  

## Summary

Monocytes (Mono) are circulating immune cells that play key roles in both immune surveillance and inflammation in human skin and other tissues. In single-cell skin atlases, the label "Mono" is consistently resolved to represent monocytes, as annotated in both adult and prenatal skin datasets. Key markers defining this population include CD14, LYZ, and FCN1, all associated with monocyte identity and function. Although predominantly localized to the peripheral blood, monocytes can enter the skin during inflammation, injury, or disease, where they may differentiate into other myeloid cell types such as macrophages or dendritic cells. Monocytes are central to initiating, regulating, and resolving immune responses and contribute to wound healing, immune homeostasis, and adaptation to tissue-specific cues.

## Markers

Several canonical markers define monocytes, with functional relevance in immune response and tissue recruitment:

- **CD14**: A co-receptor for the detection of bacterial lipopolysaccharide, essential for innate immune activation. In the skin atlas, CD14 is explicitly noted as a defining marker for the monocyte cluster (Supplementary Table 3; Name Resolution evidence).
- **LYZ (Lysozyme)**: An antimicrobial enzyme crucial for the breakdown of bacterial cell walls, marking phagocytic capabilities of classical monocytes (Supplementary Table 3; differential expression confirmed in Supplementary Table 4).
- **FCN1 (Ficolin-1)**: A molecule involved in innate immune activation and pathogen recognition, highlighted as a monocyte-specific gene from differential expression analysis (Supplementary Table 4).

Additional markers reported in the literature:
> "Only one cluster expressed requisite monocyte markers (CD11b + CD68 – HLA-DR + CD11c –) and showed expression of both CD14 and CD16 (Fig. 7A)."  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

> "Monomacs displaying a monocyte-like molecular signature (CD14, S100A8, S100A9) comprise a mixture of Infl Mo-like MNP_1 and MNP_2 clusters identified by FCM."  
> — H. Mehta et al. (2021)

Markers such as S100A8, S100A9, and CD16, as well as HLA-DR and CD38, are used to define functional and phenotypic subsets of monocytes in skin inflammation and disease.

## Location

### Blood and Skin

Monocytes are predominantly found in the peripheral blood and are rare in healthy skin under non-inflammatory conditions (Supplementary Table 2). However, they are recruited into the skin in response to injury, inflammation, or pathological conditions:

> "Monocytes are predominantly located in the peripheral blood compartment and are rare in tissue samples."  
> — Supplementary Table 2, atlas evidence

> "monos were enriched in Ldermis and L-tunnel regions, monocyte-derived macrophages (mono-macs) were enriched in L-epidermis regions"  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

> "acute inflammation and destruction of eLCs in murine models of GVHD or ultraviolet irradiation triggers an influx of monocytes to the epidermis (4,(30)(31)(32)."  
> — Anna Appios et al. (2024)

### Spatial Microenvironments

Monocytes and their derivatives are enriched in specific microenvironments of lesional (diseased) skin:

> "Monocytes and mono-macs colocalized to EC microenvironments that were functionally distinct from perilesional EC microenvironments."  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

CD38+ monocytes preferentially traffic from the bloodstream into inflamed or lesional skin regions, as shown by mass cytometry and imaging studies.

### Developmental Timing

During early stages of wound healing or in disease states, monocytes from the blood are rapidly recruited to the skin, often becoming the dominant infiltrating population at sites of injury or inflammation (A. Tsepkolenko et al., 2019).

## Function

Monocytes have multiple, context-dependent functions in the skin and systemically.

### Immune Surveillance and Homeostasis

> "The mononuclear phagocytic system contains macrophages and monocytes, where the former is found in tissues and the latter in circulation."  
> — A. Tsepkolenko et al. (2019)

Monocytes patrol the bloodstream and, during steady-state, are limited in tissue residency. They contribute to immune surveillance and, through migration, supply macrophages to tissues as needed.

### Inflammation and Tissue Recruitment

Upon injury, infection, or chronic inflammation, monocytes are recruited to the skin:

> "The observed decrease in circulating C.monos, NK cells, and DCs is associated with immune in ltration into HS L-skin."  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

> "Monocytes circulate in the blood and can migrate into tissues to become macrophages, especially during inflammation."  
> — A. Tsepkolenko et al. (2019)

This migration is largely mediated by chemokine receptors such as CCR2, and the recruited monocytes can differentiate into effector cells driving inflammation or tissue repair.

### Differentiation into Other Lineages

Monocytes in the skin can differentiate into monocyte-derived macrophages (M2 or M1 polarization), inflammatory dendritic cells, or Langerhans cell precursors, depending on microenvironmental cues:

> "By tracking monocyte differentiation, we demonstrated that epidermal monocytes undergo differentiation to EpCAM + CD207 neg precursors, which become long-lived monocyte-derived LCs (mLCs) that are transcriptionally similar to the cells they replace, are radioresistant, and acquire DClike functions of migration to LNs and priming of T cells (32)."  
> — Anna Appios et al. (2024)

> "all monocytes switched from a CD11chigh/CD14high/CD68low to a CD11chigh/CD14low/CD68high phenotype."  
> — G. C. Limandjaja et al. (2019)

In contexts such as tissue repair, monocytes tend to polarize toward M2 macrophages, while in inflamed or lesional skin they may contribute to pro-inflammatory macrophage or dendritic cell pools.

### Disease Associations and Dysregulated Function

Altered frequencies and phenotypic changes in monocyte subsets have been observed in diseases such as hidradenitis suppurativa:

> "We found no difference in the total monocyte frequency in the blood of patients with HS relative to HC (Fig. 2E). Further analyzing subsets based on the expression of CD14 and CD16, we found signi cantly decreased levels of C.monos and NC.monos and an increased level of I.monos in HS (Fig. 2E)."  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

> "CD38 expression was signi cantly higher in lesional HS skin than in perilesional skin, and the genes that were signi cantly correlated with CD38 expression were enriched for those expressed by C.monos and NK cells."  
> — Q-S Mi et al. (2023)

These changes reflect recruitment of specific monocyte subsets and functional adaptation in diseased tissue.
