# Erythroid (Fetal) in Prenatal Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: fetal
Cell Ontology: [erythroblast](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000765) (CL:0000765, broad match -- no CL term for fetal-specific erythroblast)

## Summary

Erythroid (fetal) cells are a population of definitive erythroid cells identified in the prenatal human skin atlas (Gopee et al., 2024), distinguished from the embryonic erythroid populations by their expression of fetal hemoglobin (HbF). These cells derive from definitive haematopoiesis in the fetal liver and represent erythroblasts that express the fetal globin genes HBG1 and HBG2 (gamma-globin) alongside HBA1 (alpha-globin). Popescu et al. (2019) demonstrated the temporal shift from embryonic to fetal hemoglobin expression in developing haematopoietic tissues. Fetal erythroid cells become the dominant erythroid population during the gestational window covered by the atlas (7-17 PCW), progressively replacing the primitive/embryonic erythroid wave.

## Markers

- **HBA1** (alpha-globin): Component of fetal hemoglobin (alpha2-gamma2).
- **HBG1/HBG2** (gamma-globins): Fetal beta-like globins that combine with alpha-globin to form HbF.

> "Erythroblasts show expression of haemoglobin genes, and a temporal shift from Gower 1 and 2 subunit expression (HBZ, HBE1) to fetal haemoglobin subunit expression (HBA1 and HBG2) between yolk sac and liver."
>
> -- Popescu et al. (2019)

Fetal erythroid cells are distinguished from embryonic erythroid cells by the absence of embryonic globins (HBZ, HBE1) and the predominance of fetal hemoglobin (alpha2-gamma2). The transition from embryonic to fetal globin expression coincides with the shift of haematopoiesis from the yolk sac to the fetal liver.

## Location

### Prenatal skin

Erythroid (fetal) cells are annotated in the prenatal skin atlas between 7-17 PCW (Gopee et al., 2024). They are present in the developing vasculature and the haematopoietic progenitor compartment of fetal skin.

### Fetal liver origin

Definitive erythropoiesis is centred in the fetal liver during this developmental window. Fetal erythroid cells produced in the liver enter the circulation and are detected in peripheral tissues including skin (Popescu et al., 2019). By approximately 10-11 weeks of gestation, red cell production has largely switched to the fetal liver, where HbF is the predominant hemoglobin.

## Function

### Oxygen transport

Fetal hemoglobin (HbF, alpha2-gamma2) has a higher oxygen affinity than adult hemoglobin (HbA, alpha2-beta2), which facilitates oxygen transfer from maternal to fetal blood across the placenta. This is a critical adaptation for prenatal life, ensuring adequate oxygenation of fetal tissues.

### Definitive erythropoiesis

Fetal erythroid cells represent the definitive wave of erythropoiesis, producing enucleated red blood cells (in contrast to the nucleated erythroblasts of primitive erythropoiesis). These cells undergo terminal erythropoiesis through proerythroblast, basophilic, polychromatophilic, and orthochromatic erythroblast stages, culminating in enucleation and reticulocyte release.

### Hemoglobin switching

The fetal-to-adult hemoglobin switch begins in the perinatal period, when bone marrow becomes the principal site of haematopoiesis and gamma-globin expression is replaced by beta-globin expression. At birth, approximately 60-80% of hemoglobin is HbF; this declines to less than 1% by 6 months of age.

## Structure / Morphology

Definitive fetal erythroblasts are smaller than primitive erythroblasts and undergo progressive maturation characterized by decreasing cell size, chromatin condensation, hemoglobin accumulation, and ultimately enucleation to produce reticulocytes and mature erythrocytes with the characteristic biconcave disc shape.
