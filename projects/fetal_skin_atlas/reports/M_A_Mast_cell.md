# Mast Cell (M_A_Mast_cell)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Mast cells are specialized immune cells of myeloid origin found widely throughout adult human skin, where they are particularly known for their roles in inflammation, immunity, and tissue remodeling. The adult skin mast cell population can be precisely identified by the expression of canonical markers such as CPA3, TPSAB1, and TPSB2, as confirmed through differential expression analysis and cluster annotation in the referenced skin atlas. Mast cells are predominantly tissue-resident, localized within the dermis, often in close proximity to blood vessels, nerves, and lymphatic structures, with nonuniform distribution across the skin. Their well-characterized functional attributes include participation in allergic responses, wound healing, and modulation of inflammatory states. Importantly, mast cell biology in the skin is dynamically regulated across development and aging, impacting processes such as scar formation, photoaging, and immune surveillance.

## Markers

Mast cells in adult skin are distinctly marked by high expression of several key genes and proteins, the functional relevance of which is well supported by both atlas and cross-study evidence:

- **CPA3 (Carboxypeptidase A3)**: Identified by differential expression analysis (Supplementary Table S3), CPA3 encodes a granule-associated protease critical for the processing of mast cell mediators (10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x).
- **TPSAB1 (Tryptase alpha/beta 1) and TPSB2 (Tryptase beta 2)**: Also revealed by DE analysis (Supplementary Table S3), these serine proteases are major components of mast cell granules, extensively used for immunohistochemical identification, and are pivotal in mediating the proteolytic functions of mast cells in tissue remodeling and inflammation (10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x).
- **CD117 (c-kit)** and **FcεRI (high-affinity IgE receptor)**: Surface markers routinely expressed on mast cells, vital for growth factor signaling and activation respectively. Their expression distinguishes mast cells from other leukocyte subsets (Mamazhakypov et al., 2024; Schuster et al., 2012).
- **CD45:** The leukocyte common antigen found on all hematopoietic cells, further confirming the myeloid origin (Schuster et al., 2012).
- **Chymase (CMA1/CHYM)**: Another important granule-associated protease often used to subclassify mast cells, though specific reference in atlas DE tables was not provided, it is widely recognized in histological and immunochemical detection (Schuster et al., 2012; Katinios et al., 2020).
- **Histamine, Heparin, TNF-α:** These granule-stored mediators are identifiable with specific staining and antibody-based techniques, and their presence directly relates to mast cell effector function (Herrera-Heredia et al., 2022).

Immunohistochemical and histochemical stains, such as toluidine blue and tryptase-directed antibodies, are also standard in mast cell detection due to the unique granule properties associated with these markers (Schuster et al., 2012; Katinios et al., 2020).

## Location

### Cutaneous Distribution

Atlas evidence places adult mast cells in the dermal compartment of the skin (10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x). Additional studies provide detailed spatial context:

> "In the subepidermal and perivascular compartment of the skin, mast cells were seen as individual cells but more often as cell clusters."
>
> — Czogała et al. (2011)

> "skins of the mice showed epidermal hyperplasia patches and an intense influx of inflammatory cells in the dermis, especially eosinophils and mast cells, when compared to control groups (naive and sham) (Figure 2a,b)."
>
> — Corrêa et al. (2021)

> "Investigation of glabrous and hairy skin samples by transmission electron microscopy confirmed the presence of mast cells in the dermis, adjacent to nerve fascicles and blood vessels. Often the distance between mast cells and nervous or vascular structures was less than 1-3 µm."
>
> — Caruntu et al. (2014)

> "the mast cells were found nonuniformly distributed in the dorsal skin and the data summarized in Fig. 7 A comprise an average for 15 consecutive fields from the skin of each mouse, some with numerous mast cells and others with very few."
>
> — Hart et al. (1998)

### Developmental Timing and Aging

Mast cell abundance, distribution, and maturation shift substantially throughout development and aging:

> "Marked differences were observed in the number, size, granularity and maturity of mast cells... Individual mast cells in E15 dermal tissue were significantly smaller in size (Figure 1b) and less granular (Figure 1c) than those of E18 skin, suggesting that the mast cells in E15 skin may be less mature."
>
> — Wulff et al. (2011)

Age-related increases in mast cell density are observed, particularly in photoexposed regions of the skin, correlating with photoaging and inflammatory signatures:

> "a significantly higher number of mast cells was recorded in the skin of the photoexposed compared to photoprotected region."
>
> — Leštarević et al. (2024)

> "The histochemical results support a significant increase in the number of mast cells in the dermis of elderly donors, confirming that skin aging induces inflammation."
>
> — Rezzani et al. (2024)

> "mast cells were more numerous in aged vs. young skin by 40%, most of which were located in the papillary dermis, with a lower incidence of degranulation (122)."
>
> — Gao et al. (2024)

### Pathological Accumulation

In disease states like mastocytosis, mast cell abundance can increase dramatically:

> "The pathological substrate of lesions in diffuse cutaneous mastocytosis and mastocytoma was a massive infiltrate of mast cells in the papillary and reticular layers of the dermis."
>
> — Kasikhina et al. (2023)

> "Mastocytosis (M) represents a systemic pathology characterized by increased accumulation and clonal proliferation of mast cells in the skin and/or different organs."
>
> — Șandru et al. (2021)

## Function

### Allergic and Inflammatory Responses

Mast cells are central to IgE-mediated hypersensitivity and allergic inflammation:

> "Mast cells are major effector cells of immunoglobulin (Ig) E-mediated allergic inflammatory reactions."
>
> — Park & Cho (2017)

They contribute to acute and chronic inflammatory responses in skin diseases (e.g., atopic dermatitis):

> "In the AD group, several mast cells sh a weaker staining, suggesting greater cell activation with the consequent release of mediators from cytoplasmic granules compared to the control condition (Figure 2c,d)."
>
> — Corrêa et al. (2021)

> "T quantification of intact and degranulated mast cells in the dermis confirmed the histological observations. AD skin samples showed a significant increase in degranulated m cells compared to controls, while the number of intact cells was similar between the different experimental groups (Figure 2e,f)."
>
> — Corrêa et al. (2021)

Mast cells interact with nerves and blood vessels, regulating vascular permeability, local blood flow, and neurogenic inflammation (Caruntu et al., 2014; Schuster et al., 2012).

### Wound Healing and Tissue Remodeling

> "Evidence has been mounting in support of a role for mast cells in various phases of wound healing in adult skin, especially during the inflammatory phase."
>
> — Wulff et al. (2011)

Mast cell degranulation delivers mediators (histamine, proteases) that orchestrate the transition from acute inflammation to tissue repair and fibrotic scar formation (Wulff et al., 2011; Park & Cho, 2017).

### Immune Surveillance and Host Defense

> "Mast cells regulate defense by acting as innate immune cells, interacting with the specific immune system, and inducing and regulating inflammation. Lastly, they regulate homeostasis through critical contributions to tissue remodeling, including wound healing (4)."
>
> — Park & Cho (2017)

Mast cells are strategically positioned at environmental interfaces, enabling rapid detection and response to pathogens and noxious stimuli.

### Aging, Photoaging, and Disease

Increased mast cell density with aging or chronic photoexposure is implicated in the deterioration of dermal structure, inflammation, and increased risk for skin cancers:

> "This situation shows that mast cells play an important role in skin damage caused by age-related changes. Therefore, mast cells should be considered an important target for anti-aging therapy, as reported by Gunin et al. (2011) [36]."
>
> — Rezzani et al. (2024)

> "Age-related higher accumulation of dermal mast cells in photoexposed skin can be an important factor in the photoaging process, as well as the contributing factor in the occurrence of skin cancer."
>
> — Leštarević et al. (2024)

## Structure / Morphology

Mast cells are large, round to oval mononuclear cells with numerous cytoplasmic granules that stain metachromatically with cationic dyes like toluidine blue:

> "Mast cells in the skin section were stained with toluidine blue."
>
> — Liu et al. (2013)

Their granules contain a heterogeneous assortment of proteases, amines, and cytokines, visualizable via light and electron microscopy:

> "The most characteristic feature of mature mast cells is cytoplasmic granules, which contain numerous preformed mediators that are released at the time of stimulation. Proteoglycans within the mast cell granule permit the binding of a variety of cationic dyes, such as toluidine blue or Giemsa, which stain the mast cell metachromatically."
>
> — Schuster et al. (2012)

> "mast cells had a normal appearance, with numerous intracytoplasmic electron-dense granules, uniform appearance, round-oval shape, and multiple cytoplasmic extensions"
>
> — Caruntu et al. (2014)

> "Secretory granules were either distributed at the margins of the cells or filled densely the whole cell obscuring the cell nucleus."
>
> — Czogała et al. (2011)

Different granule patterns (marginated, dispersed, or empty) are associated with stages of activation and degranulation. Patterns such as anaphylactic and piecemeal degranulation are observed in both physiological and pathological states:

> "Electron microscopic studies revealed that mast cells from canine skin with atopic dermatitis exhibited piecemeal (PMD), anaphylactic and mixed pattern of granules release."
>
> — Czogała et al. (2011)

Mast cells often appear as individuals or in clusters, especially in perivascular dermal regions (Czogała et al., 2011), and their density and morphology can be altered in disease or with age (Kasikhina et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024).
