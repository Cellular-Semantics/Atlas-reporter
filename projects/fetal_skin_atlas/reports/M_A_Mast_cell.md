# Mature/Activated Mast Cell (M_A_Mast_cell) in Adult Human Skin

Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x))
Scope: adult
Source dataset: Reynolds et al. (2021), integrated into Gopee et al. (2024)
Cell Ontology: [connective tissue type mast cell](http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/CL_0000484) (CL:0000484, exact match)

## Summary

M_A_Mast_cell represents the mature/activated mast cell population of adult human skin, as identified in Reynolds et al. (2021) and integrated into Gopee et al. (2024). Skin mast cells are predominantly of the connective tissue type (MC_TC), characterised by co-expression of tryptase (TPSAB1), chymase (CMA1), and carboxypeptidase A3 (CPA3), distinguishing them from the mucosal type (MC_T) that lacks chymase. The skin mast cell proteome is notable for uniquely high CPA3 expression relative to mast cells of other tissues (lung, gut). Mast cells are located in the superficial and deep dermis, preferentially in perivascular and periaxonal niches, where they act as sentinel cells responding to tissue damage, pathogens, and allergens through rapid degranulation and release of preformed mediators.

## Markers

Canonical connective tissue mast cell (MCTC) markers:
- **TPSAB1** — alpha/beta tryptase; canonical mast cell marker and serine protease
- **CMA1** — chymase; connective tissue mast cell-defining protease
- **CPA3** — carboxypeptidase A3; highly expressed in skin MCTC, tissue-specific marker
- **KIT (CD117)** — stem cell factor receptor; mast cell surface marker required for development and survival
- **FCER1A** — high-affinity IgE receptor alpha chain; IgE-mediated activation

Dobrican et al. (2022) describe the tissue-type distribution of mast cell subtypes:

> "The majority of tryptase-rich mast cells are predominantly located in mucous membranes (T-mast cells), while those containing tryptase, carboxypeptidase and chymase predominate in the connective tissue of the skin (TC-mast cells)"
>
> — Dobrican et al. (2022)

Siddhuraj et al. (2022) confirm the unique skin mast cell CPA3 profile:

> "tryptase and chymase have been the most studied and are also used for the classical division of mast cells into \"connective tissue-like\" mast cells (MC TC cells; containing tryptase and chymase) and \"mucosal-like\" mast cells (MC T; lacking chymase)"
>
> — Siddhuraj et al. (2022)

> "Contrasting the lung and gut, skin mast cells had a very high CPA3-staining intensity, a feature also seen among the few MCTs present in skin"
>
> — Siddhuraj et al. (2022)

## Location

Mast cells reside in the superficial and deep dermis, with preferential localisation around blood vessels and nerve axons:

> "Mast cells in patients with urticaria, similar to those in healthy individuals, are located in the superficial and deep dermis, preferentially in the perivascular and periaxonal spaces"
>
> — Dobrican et al. (2022)

This perivascular and periaxonal distribution positions mast cells to sense vascular leakage, neuropeptide release (e.g., substance P), and tissue damage, enabling rapid sentinel responses. Mast cells are also enriched near dermal appendages.

## Function

### 1. Immediate hypersensitivity and IgE-mediated activation

Skin mast cells express the high-affinity IgE receptor (FCER1A) and are the primary effectors of IgE-mediated allergic responses in the skin. Crosslinking of IgE-bound FCER1A by allergens triggers rapid degranulation, releasing preformed mediators including histamine, tryptase, chymase, and CPA3.

### 2. Protease-mediated tissue remodelling and defence

The mast cell-specific proteases — tryptase (TPSAB1), chymase (CMA1), and carboxypeptidase A3 (CPA3) — cleave a broad range of substrates including ECM proteins, neuropeptides, cytokines, and complement components. These proteases contribute to tissue remodelling, host defence, and regulation of local inflammatory responses:

> "mast cells may act in a pro-inflammatory manner by releasing histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, and cytokines. Another important mechanism by which mast cells influence their tissue surroundings is through their specific proteases."
>
> — Siddhuraj et al. (2022)

### 3. Neuro-immune communication

The periaxonal localisation of dermal mast cells positions them as key mediators of neuro-immune crosstalk. Mast cells respond to neuropeptides released by sensory nerve fibres (e.g., substance P, CGRP) and in turn release mediators that modulate neuronal activity, contributing to itch and pain in inflammatory skin conditions.

## Disease relevance

Dermal mast cells are central to the pathogenesis of urticaria, atopic dermatitis, and mastocytosis. In chronic spontaneous urticaria, mast cell degranulation drives wheal-and-flare responses. In atopic dermatitis, mast cell-derived IL-33 responders and histamine contribute to itch amplification. Reynolds et al. (2021) include mast cells as part of the broader adult skin immune landscape integrated into Gopee et al. (2024), and the M_A (mature/activated) designation indicates a fully differentiated, granule-bearing state.
