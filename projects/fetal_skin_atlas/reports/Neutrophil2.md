## Summary

Neutrophil2 is one of two transcriptionally distinct neutrophil subpopulations annotated in fetal human skin (7–17 PCW) by the Gopee et al. (2024) prenatal skin atlas. Together with Neutrophil1 it completes the fetal skin neutrophil compartment. The atlas confirmed the broad timing of innate immune cell appearance in fetal skin:

> "Innate immune cells, such as macrophages and innate lymphoid cells (ILCs), were present from early gestation, whereas B cells and T cells emerged later, accompanying thymus, bone marrow and spleen formation from around 10 PCW"
> — Gopee et al. (2024), *Nature*

A neutrophil-myeloid precursor population was also noted in first-trimester skin:

> "plasmacytoid DCs, monocytes, monocyte-DCs (having both a monocyte and DC gene signature) and a neutrophil-myeloid precursor have also recently been identified in first-trimester human skin using single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNAseq)."
> — Botting and Haniffa (2020), *Immunology*

Neutrophil2 is distinguished from Neutrophil1 by differentially expressed genes in Supplementary Table 3 of the atlas. The subpopulation likely represents a distinct maturation state, activation phenotype, or tissue-adaptation program. CXCL8, produced by prenatal keratinocytes, may serve as a chemoattractant guiding neutrophil positioning within the fetal dermis.

## Markers

Marker genes distinguishing Neutrophil2 from Neutrophil1 are detailed in Supplementary Table 3 of Gopee et al. (2024). Canonical neutrophil markers relevant to maturation status include SELL, CXCR1, CXCR2, FCGR3B, S100A8, and S100A9. Differential expression of maturity markers likely underlies the separation of the two fetal skin neutrophil subpopulations.

## Location

Neutrophil2 cells are found within fetal skin (dermis/immune compartment) during prenatal development (7–17 PCW). They were isolated as CD45+ immune cells by FACS prior to scRNA-seq.

## Function

Fetal neutrophil function is generally immature compared to adult counterparts:

> "Mature neutrophils are present at the end of the first trimester and steeply increase in number, stimulated by granulocyte-colony-stimulating factor, shortly before birth. Their number then returns to a stable level within days, but they show weak bactericidal functions, poor responses to inflammatory stimuli, reduced adhesion to endothelial cells and diminished chemotaxis"
> — Simon et al. (2015), *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*

Neutrophil-biased myeloid differentiation during late fetal development is partly G-CSF-dependent:

> "neutrophil-biased differentiation was partially mediated by G-CSF"
> — Ishiwata and Morimoto (2024), *bioRxiv*

The distinct transcriptional profile of Neutrophil2 relative to Neutrophil1 may reflect a specific tissue-adapted or more mature state within the fetal skin neutrophil compartment.

## Structure / Morphology

No specific structural data for fetal skin Neutrophil2 are reported in the atlas text. The neutrophil maturation series spans from myeloblast through promyelocyte, myelocyte, metamyelocyte, band cell to segmented neutrophil stages, with scRNA-seq data capturing transcriptional states across this spectrum.
